# HormonaIQ — Copy Guidelines
**For writers, designers, and anyone who puts words in the product**
Version 1.0 | April 2026

---

> Before you write a single word of HormonaIQ copy, internalize this: the person reading it has probably spent years not being believed. She has done her own research. She has prepared for doctor's appointments. She has been called "very in tune with her body" in a tone that meant something far less kind. She is intelligent, she is exhausted, and she has a finely tuned detector for condescension.
>
> If what you've written sounds like it came from a hospital pamphlet, a wellness brand, or someone who has never had a bad week in their life — it's wrong. Start again.

## SECTION 1 — Voice and Tone Foundation

### The Voice: Who HormonaIQ Is

HormonaIQ is not a brand speaking. It is a person. Specifically, it is a close friend who also happens to have a medical degree, years of research experience in women's hormonal health, and absolutely no patience for telling you things are fine when they're not.

Here is the character in full:

**She has done the work.** She knows the clinical literature. She knows what PMDD actually is. She knows the diagnostic criteria, the DRSP, the average diagnostic delay. She uses clinical terms correctly and explains them without being condescending. She treats you as an adult who can handle accurate information.

**She does not perform warmth.** She is warm, but not demonstratively. The warmth shows up in what she says, not in exclamation points and emoji. She doesn't tell you you're amazing. She shows up, consistently, at the hard moments, with something true and useful to say.

**She will name the hard thing.** She will say "this is genuinely difficult" when it is. She will say "your data shows something concerning" when it does. She does not round the edges off reality to make you feel better in the short term. She knows that the women who need this app have been soothed enough — they need accuracy.

**She is on your side.** Not neutral. Not a passive information provider. On your side. She is helping you build a case. She is helping you walk into a doctor's office with something undeniable. She is doing this because you have been doing it alone for too long.

**She knows when to be quiet.** She does not fill silence. She does not send you something just because it's been a few days. When she reaches out, it is because she has something worth saying. She never reaches out just to stay top-of-mind.

### 5 Tone Dimensions

---

**Dimension 1: Honest, Not Harsh**

The truth, delivered with care. The goal is accuracy — not brutal frankness, not cheerful softening. The right balance is: "here is what your data shows, and here is what it might mean for you."

Before:
> "You're in luteal phase, which some people find challenging! Try to be extra gentle with yourself this week."

After:
> "You're in luteal phase. For a lot of people with PMDD, this is the hard stretch. Your data suggests it starts around now for you."

The difference: the "before" is soft and slightly patronizing. The "after" trusts her with specific information and respects what she already knows.

---

**Dimension 2: Direct, Not Blunt**

Short sentences. Clear meaning. No hedging language that dilutes the message. But also: not cold. There is room in directness for care — the care is in the precision, not in effusive language.

Before:
> "You might want to consider reaching out to a healthcare provider about some of the patterns your data is showing, as they could potentially be useful in informing your care."

After:
> "This is worth bringing to your doctor. We've prepared a summary you can share."

---

**Dimension 3: Clinical, Not Cold**

We use the correct medical and clinical terms. PMDD, not "hormonal mood disorder." DRSP, not "daily symptom checker." Luteal phase, not "the week before your period." We explain terms where explanation is needed, but we do not substitute vague language for correct language.

Before:
> "Your symptoms often get more intense in the two weeks before your period."

After:
> "Your DRSP scores show consistent elevation in the luteal phase, typically starting around day 19 for you."

---

**Dimension 4: Present, Not Projected**

We write in the present tense, about what is happening now and what has happened. We do not project emotions or futures. We do not tell her how she will feel. We observe and report.

Before:
> "This week might be tough, but you'll come out the other side feeling so much better."

After:
> "This week is typically your hardest stretch based on your last three cycles."

---

**Dimension 5: Earned, Not Assumed**

We do not assume intimacy. We earn it, slowly, through consistency and accuracy. Early interactions are respectful and somewhat formal — still human, but not presumptuously close. As the app accumulates data and the user's pattern becomes known, the voice becomes more specific and more companioning.

Day 1:
> "You're heading into the luteal phase. For many people with PMDD, this phase brings increased symptom severity."

Day 60:
> "Day 22 — this is usually your hardest stretch. Your data shows you typically start feeling better around day 27."

The second version is only possible because time and data have been earned. It would feel presumptuous on day 1. It feels like genuine companionship on day 60.

---

### The Honest Friend Test

Every piece of copy in this product should pass this test:

**Would a brilliant friend who happened to have a medical degree in women's health actually say this to you?**

If the answer is no — if it sounds too clinical, too warm, too cheery, too vague, too corporate, too much like a hospital form, too much like a wellness brand — it fails the test.

The test works in both directions:
- "You're doing so great with your tracking!" — fails the test (no one actually talks like this)
- "Progesterone levels typically decline in the late luteal phase, which can cause dysregulation of serotonergic activity." — fails the test (too clinical, too lecture-mode)
- "This is usually the hardest week. Your data says day 22. You're on day 21." — passes

### What We Never Say — The Full Banned List

These phrases are banned. They have explanations because banning without explanation breeds workarounds. If you understand why a phrase is banned, you can avoid the thinking that produces it.

**"Balance your hormones"**
Why banned: Medically meaningless. Hormones are in constant flux — "balance" implies a static ideal that doesn't exist. This language belongs to supplement brands selling things to women who have real conditions. It trivializes the complexity of what our users are managing.

**"Listen to your body"**
Why banned: Profoundly condescending to a woman who has been listening obsessively to her body for years and was told by doctors that her observations were wrong. This phrase puts the burden back on her and implies that she simply hasn't been paying attention.

**"Period tracker" (applied to HormonaIQ)**
Why banned: We are not a period tracker. Period tracking is one data input. Calling us a period tracker tells our users that we are exactly what we are not. It is the single most effective way to end a conversation with the PMDD, PCOS, or perimenopause patient who came here for something different.

**"Wellness journey"**
Why banned: "Journey" implies leisurely progress toward a destination. Our users are not on a journey. They are managing a condition. They want accurate information and clinical documentation, not a metaphor for growth. "Wellness" is the category we are explicitly not in.

**"Take control of your health"**
Why banned: It implies that the reason she doesn't have answers yet is that she hasn't taken enough control. She has been taking all the control she can. The problem was never her effort level — the problem was a medical system that failed her. This phrase lets the system off the hook.

**"You deserve to feel good"**
Why banned: True but inadequate. She doesn't need to be told she deserves anything — she needs information, documentation, and a tool that works. "You deserve to feel good" is what you say instead of help. We provide help.

**"We're here for you"**
Why banned (as standalone): Too vague to mean anything. Show up through specific, useful, timely actions. Don't announce presence — demonstrate it.

**"I hear you" / "Your feelings are valid"**
Why banned: The validation-speak of wellness and therapy apps. These women have heard "your feelings are valid" from practitioners who then moved on without doing anything. It has been so overused in the contexts they inhabit that it has become a signal of non-action.

**"Luminous" / "Glowing" / "Radiant"**
Why banned: The register of cycle apps that map fertility to emotional peak experiences. Flo says this. We are the opposite of Flo.

**"Self-care"**
Why banned: Overloaded with the wellness-industry meaning. When someone with PMDD is suicidal in luteal phase, being told to practice self-care is insulting. The word has been used to package inadequate support as individual empowerment.

**Any exclamation point**
Why banned: Exclamation points belong to a register of enthusiasm and encouragement that is not ours. The correct emotional register is steady, precise, grounded. Steadiness does not exclaim. This is a hard rule with no exceptions.

**"Almost there!" / "You've got this!"**
Why banned: Cheerleading. She is not in a race. She is managing a condition. The presence of cheerleading copy in her health tool tells her we don't understand what she's actually going through.

**"Some women experience..." (as evasion)**
Why banned when used to soften: Accuracy over diplomacy. If the data shows she experiences this, say so. The vague collective is a way of avoiding the specific, and the specific is what she needs.

### What We Always Say — Required Elements for Sensitive Communications

When writing for any sensitive moment (high distress, crisis-adjacent, escalating severity, post-crisis return), the following elements must be present:

**1. Acknowledgment before anything else.** The experience is named before information is given, resources are offered, or action is suggested. One sentence. Direct. No embellishment.

**2. Data grounding where available.** Connect what she is experiencing to her own data. "Your data shows this" is more powerful than "this is common" because it is specifically about her, not about a population she may or may not feel part of.

**3. One next step, not a list.** At high-distress moments, cognitive capacity is limited. One clear, optional next step. Not three suggestions, not a list of resources, not a paragraph of options.

**4. The door stays open.** Any sensitive communication ends with something that indicates the app will be there when she comes back. Not in a demanding way. Not with a prompt or a CTA. A simple, quiet indication of continuity.

**5. No self-congratulation.** The app never thanks itself for reaching out. It does not say "we're so glad you logged today." It does not congratulate her for opening the app. The communication is about her, not about the relationship between her and the app.

## SECTION 2 — Phase Copy Library

### How to Use This Library

These are not templates to be used verbatim indefinitely. They are models — both for the tone and the approach that should inform AI-generated or writer-generated variations. The goal is to have enough variety that no user sees the same message twice in the same cycle, across cycles.

Each phase section contains a 7-day rotation of messages. The variants are labeled Day A through Day G for tracking purposes. Within a given cycle, messages should not repeat. Across cycles, variation should be maximized.

## SECTION 3 — Motivational Copy That Actually Works

### Why Standard Wellness Affirmations Fail Here

Affirmations work by telling the recipient something positive about themselves or their situation. They assume a baseline of receptivity — that the person is open to feeling better.

For someone in luteal peak who has been managing a debilitating condition alone for years, affirmations are not received as uplifting. They are received as evidence that the person speaking does not understand what she is going through. "You are strong and capable" is what you say to someone who has temporarily forgotten her strength. It is not what you say to someone who is accurately perceiving that her brain chemistry is doing something brutal to her for the tenth consecutive month.

What motivates these women is not encouragement. It is:

**Validation first.** Acknowledging that what is hard is actually hard, not reframed as growth.

**Evidence.** Data that confirms her pattern, shows her where she is relative to previous cycles, and gives her factual grounds to believe it will end.

**Specificity.** The more specific the acknowledgment ("your data shows this is typically your hardest four days"), the more it lands. Vague encouragement feels hollow. Specific, accurate information feels like being seen.

**Agency over performance.** Not "you can do this" but "here's what the data shows you've already done." The shift from future encouragement to past evidence is critical.

### The Earned Celebration Model

Celebration in HormonaIQ is reserved, earned, and specific. It is not for logging streaks. It is not for completing onboarding. It is not for using the app for 30 days.

Celebration is for:

**Milestones that are actually significant:**
- First complete DRSP cycle (two full cycles of daily logging — the diagnostic standard)
- First exported clinical summary
- First doctor appointment where she brought her data
- First pattern identified by Ora
- Six months of continuous data

**Survived hard moments:**
- After a particularly high-severity luteal window has ended
- When the app detects the symptom relief of menstruation following a long luteal stretch

**How to celebrate:**

Not confetti. Not badges. Not streaks.

A single, calm, specific acknowledgment. Written in the same voice as everything else. Not louder, not more exclamatory, not in a different design system.

For first complete DRSP cycle:
> "You've completed two full cycles of DRSP data. That meets the prospective tracking standard referenced in PMDD diagnostic guidelines. This is clinical documentation. You can export it now."

For a hard window ending:
> "The harder stretch is lifting. You tracked through it. That data is yours."

For first appointment with data:
> "You brought your data to your appointment. That conversation is different from every one that came before it."

The tone of celebration is: quiet pride, earned. Not confetti. Not exuberance. The recognition that something real was done.

### Scenario 1: First Time Logging Suicidal Ideation

Context: A user logs suicidal ideation for the first time. The app has no prior pattern data on this user's severity.

**Tier response: Tier 2 (outside established pattern, first occurrence)**

Logging acknowledgment (appears after log is completed, not during):
> "Logged. This is one of the symptoms some people experience during PMDD's harder phases. We have it recorded. If you'd like to talk to someone, that's easy to reach from here."

Beneath this: a quiet text link labeled "Connect with support" — not a button, not prominent, not labeled "Crisis Hotline."

Check-in message (sent 15 minutes after the log):
> "That was a harder log than most. Are you okay right now?"

Three response options:
- "I'm okay"
- "It's a hard day"
- "I need help now"

If "I'm okay":
> "Okay. We've got it logged. Reach out any time."

If "It's a hard day":
> "That's real. Hard days during this phase are documented and consistent with PMDD. If you need support right now, it's one tap away."
> [Support resources — listed simply, not alarmingly]

If "I need help now": Tier 3 screen activates (see below).

---

### Scenario 2: User Hasn't Logged in 7 Days During a Predicted Hard Phase

Context: The app's cycle model predicts the user is in their luteal peak. They have not logged for 7 days.

**Copy:**

Notification (sent once, not repeated):
> "You haven't logged in a few days. No pressure — we're here when you're ready."

If the user opens the app after receiving this:
Home screen shows the regular phase message with one addition beneath it:
> "Welcome back. Your data is here whenever you're ready to log."

No mention of the absence. No reference to the gap. No streak break notification. The data simply continues from where it left off.

If the user opens the log for the first time after the gap:
The logging screen opens normally. After completing the log, a single quiet message appears:
> "Logged. You're back."

Nothing more.

---

### Scenario 3: User Marks 5/5 Distress for 3 Consecutive Days

Context: Three consecutive days of maximum distress scores. This may be within established pattern or outside it — the response differs.

**If within established pattern:**

After the third consecutive 5/5 log:
> "Three days of high severity in a row. That's documented. Your pattern shows this phase typically lasts [X] days total — you're on day [Y]. If you'd like support, it's here."
> [Single quiet link to support resources]

**If outside established pattern (higher than typical, or outside predicted window):**

Trigger Tier 2 check-in:
> "Your last three logs have been at maximum severity. That's higher than your usual pattern for this phase. Are you okay right now?"

Three response options (same as Scenario 1).

---

### Scenario 4: User's Pattern Shows Escalating Severity Across Cycles

Context: Ora has identified that the user's DRSP severity scores have been increasing over consecutive cycles. This is a clinical signal that warrants provider attention.

**The message Ora surfaces (in Insights tab):**

> "Ora has identified a pattern in your data. Your average symptom severity in the luteal phase has increased over your last [X] cycles — from [Y] to [Z] on the DRSP scale. This is something worth discussing with your healthcare provider. Your clinical summary for this pattern is ready to export."

**What the message does NOT say:**
- Does not diagnose
- Does not alarm
- Does not speculate on cause
- Does not recommend specific treatment

The message is an instrument report. Observation, not interpretation.

**If the user has an appointment on record:**
An additional line appears:
> "You have an appointment logged for [date]. We can prepare a summary for that conversation."

---

### Scenario 5: User Shares Data with a Doctor (Appointment Prep Moment)

Context: The user is preparing to export their DRSP data for a doctor's appointment.

**The export screen header:**
> "Your clinical summary is ready."

**Below the header:**
> "This document meets the prospective symptom recording standard referenced in PMDD diagnostic guidelines. It shows your daily DRSP scores, phase mapping, and Ora's pattern analysis across your logged cycles."

**Export button:**
> "Export PDF"

**Beneath the export button, small text:**
> "This is your data. You own it. We don't see it after it leaves this app unless you choose to share it with us."

**After export completes:**
> "Exported. You're going into that appointment with something real."

---

### Scenario 6: Period Arrives After Long PMDD Window

Context: The app detects (or the user logs) that their period has started after a longer-than-typical luteal stretch.

**The relief message:**
> "Your period has started. The hard part is lifting."

This message appears as a quiet banner on the home screen, not a push notification, not a celebration. It does not say more than this. It does not ask how she feels. It does not offer any actions.

If this luteal window was longer than her typical pattern:
An additional line appears below:
> "That was a longer stretch than usual. Your data has it recorded."

Nothing more is needed.

---

### Scenario 7: User Receives a Diagnosis Milestone Notification

Context: The user has flagged in their profile that they received a new diagnosis. This is an emotionally complex moment — relief, grief, anger, and vindication often coexist.

**The response:**
> "You've updated your diagnosis. That's significant. If you want to update your tracking settings to reflect this, we can do that now — or it can wait."

Two options:
- "Update settings now"
- "Later"

**What this message does NOT do:**
- Does not congratulate her on getting diagnosed (which can feel reductive about how long it took)
- Does not minimize the difficulty of the diagnostic journey
- Does not assume how she feels about the diagnosis
- Does not trigger confetti or celebration

If she selects "Update settings now," the settings are updated with a quiet confirmation:
> "Updated. Your tracking will now reflect [condition]."

## SECTION 5 — Error States and System Messages

The rule: no "Oops!" No "Something went wrong." No "Uh oh." These are wellness-app conventions that signal informality and low stakes. Our stakes are high. Our tone is steady. That includes when things break.

---

### Connectivity Errors

**No internet connection:**
> "No connection right now. Your log is saved locally and will sync when you're back online."

**Sync failed:**
> "That didn't sync — it will retry automatically. Your data is safe on your device."

---

### Data Errors

**Log failed to save:**
> "That didn't save. Try again? If it keeps happening, your data is protected and we can recover it."

**Export failed:**
> "The export didn't complete. Try again, or check your storage and connection. Your data is still here."

**Data load error:**
> "Something's taking longer than expected. Pull down to refresh."

---

### Account and Auth Errors

**Login failed:**
> "That didn't work. Check your details and try again."

**Verification failed:**
> "We couldn't verify that. Try resending."

**Session expired:**
> "You were signed out after a period of inactivity. Sign in again — your data is secure."

---

### Empty States

**No logs yet:**
> "Nothing here yet. Your first log will show up here."

**No pattern data yet:**
> "Patterns need at least two full cycles to appear. Your data is building."

**No community posts in this phase segment:**
> "No posts here yet. This space is ready when people are."

**Insights not yet available:**
> "Your insights will appear here after two complete cycles of logging."

---

### Notification Permission Denied

If the user declines notification permissions:
> "Got it. You won't receive phase alerts or check-ins. You can change this in your phone's settings whenever you're ready."

No pressure. No explanation of what they're missing. No re-prompting unless the user navigates to notification settings themselves.

## SECTION 6 — Onboarding Copy

### Design Principle for Onboarding

Onboarding is not a product tour. It is not a welcome ceremony. It is the first time she is meeting something that might, finally, take her seriously. Every screen has one job: make the next screen feel earned.

The onboarding asks for very little. It sets expectations clearly. It does not over-promise. It does not perform enthusiasm. By the end of onboarding, she should feel: this is different. This is going to work.

---

### Screen 1 — The Opening

**Headline (Display, Fraunces):**
> "You found us."

**Body:**
> "This isn't a period tracker."
> "It isn't a fertility app."
> "It's for the other weeks of the month — the ones no one else tracks."

**Button:**
> "Continue"

**Design notes:** Full bleed Cloud White. No illustration. No animation. No tagline. Just these words and space.

---

### Screen 2 — Who This Is For

**Headline:**
> "Are any of these yours?"

**Options (checkboxes, can select multiple):**
- PMDD or severe PMS
- PCOS
- Perimenopause or menopause
- Hashimoto's or thyroid conditions
- ADHD that changes with my cycle
- Endometriosis
- I don't have a diagnosis yet
- I just want to understand my patterns

**Button:**
> "Continue — we'll make this yours"

**Below the button, small text:**
> "Your answers stay on your device."

**Design notes:** Checkboxes, not icons. No illustrations of women. Clean list. The option "I don't have a diagnosis yet" is not at the bottom — it is integrated mid-list, not as an afterthought.

---

### Screen 3 — The Promise

**Headline:**
> "What we will never do."

**Body:**
> "We will never tell you you're feeling LUMINOUS when you feel like death."
>
> "We will never sell your data to anyone."
>
> "We will never pretend this is easy."
>
> "We will show up honestly. Every day."

**Button:**
> "Let's start"

**Design notes:** No bullet points. Line breaks between each promise. Each stands alone. Nothing decorative. The word LUMINOUS is the only word in all caps — it is a deliberate quotation of every app she has already tried and left.

---

### Screen 4 — One Thing to Set Up

**Headline:**
> "One thing, to start."

**Body:**
> "When did your last period begin? (Or your best guess.)"

**Input:**
Date picker — or a plain text option "I don't track periods" and "I'm not sure"

**Below the input:**
> "This is the only thing we need right now. Everything else, we learn."

**Button:**
> "That's it"

**Design notes:** No 20-question intake. No condition-specific questions yet. No symptom lists. The minimum viable start.

---

### Screen 5 — What Happens Next

**Headline:**
> "Here's how this works."

**Body (three points, each a single short paragraph):**

> "Every day, the app checks in. One question, sometimes two. It takes under a minute."

> "After two cycles, patterns start to appear. What your hardest days look like, when they arrive, and how long they last. That becomes clinical documentation."

> "You can share that documentation with a doctor when you're ready. Or not. It's yours."

**Button:**
> "Take me to the app"

## SECTION 7 — Notification Copy Library

### The Rule: The Friend Test

Before every notification goes out, apply this test: **Would a good friend actually send this message?**

A good friend does not send:
- "Don't forget to check in today!" (no reason, generic)
- "You're doing great!" (empty, context-free)
- "It's been a while since your last log. Come back!" (slightly desperate, no care for context)

A good friend does send:
- "Heads up — you're heading into your harder stretch. Tomorrow is day 22."
- "You logged through the whole luteal phase this cycle. That's a full DRSP."
- "Your period data just synced. The hardest part is behind you."

Every notification in this library has been written with that test in mind.
