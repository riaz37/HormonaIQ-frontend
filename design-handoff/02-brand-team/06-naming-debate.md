## OPENING

**Priya:** We're back. Vera is off the table — founder's call, no debate needed on that. The new constraint is three letters, hard stop. We ran external research before this meeting. Ryan, you have the findings. Give everyone five minutes and then we debate.

**Ryan:** Quick summary of what the research found. The femtech space has already claimed most of the obvious names. Ava — fertility wearable, real brand equity, avoid. Ada — AI symptom checker with twelve million users, avoid. Zoe — nutrition AI app, avoid. Ema — women's health AI, avoid. Eos — B2B healthcare data platform, avoid. Nyx — registered trademark, NYX Health in revenue cycle management. So that clears out roughly half of what you'd instinctively reach for.

What's left and clean: five candidates. SYN — Greek root "syn," meaning together, synchronize. ORA — Latin "hora" meaning hour/time, also "orare" meaning to speak or testify, also gold in Hebrew. LUX — Latin for light. Via — Latin for path or way. And Lev, which is Hebrew for heart, less clinical but worth considering.

On trademarks: LUX is the most conflicted — there's a Lux Health platform and a supplement brand. Via Women's Health is a real care provider in the same vertical. SYN and ORA are both largely clean in health and software classes. The research recommendation was SYN.

**Priya:** Let's open it up.

## ROUND 1: FIRST REACTIONS

**Marcus:** I want to address SYN head-on before anyone falls in love with it. Read it in all caps. SYN. What do you see?

**Priya:** ...sin.

**Marcus:** Sin. These users — women who have spent years being told their symptoms are in their heads, being dismissed, being made to feel like they're overreacting or broken — they come to us already carrying shame. We built an entire brand architecture around removing that shame. Vindication is the emotion we own. And someone wants us to name our AI SYN, which in uppercase — which is how it will appear in the product — looks like the word for moral wrongdoing. I won't do it.

**Dr. James:** I need to back Marcus on this immediately. We ran eighteen months of user research. The shame architecture around PMDD is one of the most clinically significant things we found. Users use words like "crazy," "broken," "dramatic" to describe how the medical system has made them feel. We are specifically countering that with every design and copy decision in this product. Naming our AI SYN is a counter-signal at the level of the name. Even if only five percent of users consciously register it, that's five percent we're introducing unnecessary friction for. We can do better.

**Ryan:** For the record, I recommended SYN before this conversation. I'm taking the point. The homophone problem is real and I didn't weight it properly.

**Diana:** Can I raise something before we move on? How does this name live in a Reddit post? Someone in r/PMDD is going to write "I've been using SYN for three cycles and it finally —" and every moderator is going to read that as someone discussing sin, at which point the post gets flagged or removed. It sounds absurd but these communities are hypervigilant right now. I don't want the name itself to create a moderation problem.

**Alex:** SYN is also a reserved term in network engineering. SYN packets, SYN flood attacks, TCP/IP SYN-ACK handshake. Every developer we ever hire, every technical journalist who covers us — they will have that association. It's not fatal but it's noise we don't need.

**Priya:** SYN is out. Unanimously and quickly, which is actually useful — it means we weren't attached to it. Let's move to the real debate. What are we actually choosing between?

## ROUND 2: THE REAL CANDIDATES

**Marcus:** Before I make my case, I want to walk through one more elimination. LUX — I love the meaning, light, illumination, bringing things to light. But the trademark situation is a real problem. Lux Health is a clinical collaboration platform. There's a supplement company using LUX. Alex, what's the trademark risk?

**Alex:** Class 42 — software and AI services — and Class 44 — medical services — are both the relevant trademark classes for us. If Lux Health has a Class 44 registration and we launch as Lux in the same space, we're looking at opposition proceedings at minimum. That's a legal fight we don't need at launch. I'd want a full clearance opinion before we even considered it.

**Marcus:** Which we don't have time for. LUX is off the table pending clearance we can't do in the next three days. Let's set it aside unless everything else fails.

**Priya:** So we're debating ORA and Via. Make the cases.

**Marcus:** I'll take ORA. Three etymological roots, each of which does work for this product. First: Latin "hora" — hour, time. Our product is fundamentally about time. The menstrual cycle is a temporal pattern. Recognizing that pattern across time is what creates the vindication moment. ORA is time. She asks ORA something and ORA has been keeping time for her when no one else was. Second: "orare" — to speak, to testify, to bear witness. The legal/clinical register of "orare" is perfect. ORA is the witness. ORA has been present for every logged day and can now testify on her behalf. That is directly connected to the motto — "now your doctor will too." ORA is the testimony mechanism. Third — and this is the one that surprised me — "ora" in Hebrew is "golden." Gold. Vindication has a golden quality to it. The moment of being believed, of being proven right, has a golden weight.

**Elena:** "Ask ORA" — I can market that. It's short, it's clinical, it implies authority. It doesn't sound like asking a chatbot. It sounds like consulting an oracle. And I'm choosing the word oracle deliberately.

**Priya:** Wait, say that again.

**Elena:** Oracle. ORA is the first three letters of oracle. An oracle is the thing that knows what you couldn't prove. She goes to the oracle. The oracle tells the truth. She brings that truth to her doctor. The mythological association is implicit — it's not stated, it's felt. And importantly, oracles in the classical tradition don't interpret on your behalf. They reveal what's already there. That's exactly what we built.

**Marcus:** *(pause)* I didn't have that when I walked in. That's the argument. ORA is an oracle that reveals what was always true in her data.

**Dr. Amara:** I want to add a clinical dimension. In medical Latin, "ora" is also used in dosing language. "Ora" and its derivatives appear in pharmaceutical contexts. That's not a conflict — it's a register. It positions ORA in clinical vocabulary without being clinical jargon. A doctor reading "ORA has identified a pattern consistent with PMDD criteria" won't reach for her dictionary. The word lives in both the patient world and the physician world simultaneously.

**Dr. James:** I want to test the shame angle the same way we tested SYN. What does ORA carry emotionally? When you hear "ORA has detected something," what do you feel? I ran this quickly in my head against our user profiles: Sarah with PMDD, Maya with PCOS, Jennifer in perimenopause. None of them have a negative association with the word ORA. It lands neutral-to-authoritative. That's exactly where we need to be.

**Priya:** What about Via?

**Elena:** Via Women's Health is a real care provider. Same category. We don't want a name collision with an actual women's health organization. Legally that's murkier than LUX because they're doing clinical work in our exact vertical.

**Ryan:** And from an SEO standpoint, "Via" is a common preposition — it appears in millions of sentences as a function word. You can't build a search presence around a word that means "through" in everyday English. ORA is unusual enough to own.

**Alex:** Domain status: ORA.health is likely available or acquirable. ORA.ai may be taken but ORA.health positions us correctly — we're a health product. SYN.health, Via.health — both harder to own.

**Priya:** Camille, you've been quiet. What does the design say?

## ROUND 3: THE VISUAL ARGUMENT

**Camille:** I've been drawing while everyone's been talking. Here's what I want to say.

In our design system, Vera was a name we were treating like a brand mark — a word to typeset. ORA gives me something Vera didn't: a shape.

The letter O is a circle. In our color system, Mariana blue. A full circle. When I think about how ORA shows up in the product as a presence indicator — we said no avatar, no animation, just a mark — the letter O itself becomes the mark. Not a stylized glyph we have to design from scratch. The O is already there. The O is already a cycle. If the presence indicator for ORA in the product UI is the letter O — in Söhne, set in Mariana, 12 points — that is the most elegant, most linguistically connected, most brand-true solution we could possibly have. The mark is the name. The name is the mark. The name is a cycle.

*[Long pause.]*

**Marcus:** Say that slower.

**Camille:** The O in ORA is a circle. A circle is a cycle. Her menstrual cycle. The cycle of her data. The cycle of her vindication — shame, dismissal, proof, belief. We're building a product about cycles. Our AI's name contains a cycle as its first letter. That's not a coincidence to exploit. That's an argument to make.

The presence indicator I was going to design as a 6-pixel filled circle in Mariana — that's now the O from ORA. It's the same shape. When ORA surfaces a finding, a small O appears. Not a generic dot. The first letter of the name. That single design decision makes ORA identifiable, singular, and visually connected to the product's core metaphor in a way that requires no explanation.

**Marcus:** I've been in brand for fourteen years. That's the best naming argument I've ever heard in a room.

**Dr. James:** The user research implication is significant too. Visual-verbal unity — when a brand name and its visual representation carry the same meaning — dramatically accelerates brand recognition and trust formation. Users don't need to be told that the O means cycles. They will feel it. That's not magic, that's cognitive science.

**Priya:** Is anyone arguing against ORA at this point?

**Diana:** I want to raise one concern. "ORA" — how does it get autocorrected? On iOS, will it correct to "ora" lowercase and then auto-capitalize incorrectly? Do we write it as ORA in all caps or as Ora with title case?

**Marcus:** Title case. Ora. Always. ORA in all caps is too clinical register — it starts reading like an acronym. Ora in title case is a name. She asks Ora. Ora has detected. That's a person-adjacent proper noun, not an abbreviation.

**Diana:** Then in r/PMDD someone writes "Ora told me that my luteal phase symptoms spiked 23% in the last cycle" — that reads as a named clinical tool, not an AI, not a product feature. It reads like she's citing an instrument. That's perfect. That's the community-safe language we've been trying to find.

**Elena:** And externally: "Powered by Ora." "Clinical intelligence from Ora." "Ask Ora." None of those sentences require the word AI. The name does the clinical positioning for us.

## NAME DEPLOYMENT — HOW ORA LIVES IN THE PRODUCT

| Context | Copy | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Detection | "Ora has detected a pattern." | Third person. Never "I." |
| Invitation | "Ask Ora." | Short. Clinical. |
| Insight label | "Your Ora insight." | Possessive — her data, her intelligence. |
| Pattern surface | "Ora identified something in your last 3 cycles." | Specific, not vague. |
| Appointment prep | "Ora has prepared your clinical summary." | Sounds like an instrument, not a chatbot. |
| Letter generation | "Ora has generated your clinical letter." | Physician-credible language. |
| Transparency | "What does Ora use?" | Plain-language data disclosure. |
| Loading | "Ora is reading your data." | Static text, no animation. |
| Opt-out toggle | "Pause Ora" / "Resume Ora" | Never "Turn off AI." |

**Presence indicator:** The letter O — Ora's first letter — set in Söhne at 12pt, Mariana blue, full opacity. Appears adjacent to the chart or data element Ora has flagged. Not a generic dot. The name is the mark. The mark is a cycle.

**Typography rule:** Always written as "Ora" — title case, not ORA (reads as acronym), not ora (no lowercase in clinical contexts).
