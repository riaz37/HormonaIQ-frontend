# F150 Late-Diagnosis Support — Brand × UX Co-Review

**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Co-reviewers:** Aria (Brand Manager) + Iris (UX Research Lead)
**File touched:** `app/src/modules-5-adhd.jsx` — `M.adhdLateDiagnosis`
**Why this co-review:** Late diagnosis is the most emotionally loaded surface in the entire ADHD module. The user comes here freshly named (often within 12 months of getting their diagnosis), grieving for the years before, and looking for words. Voice + UX both have to land on the first read. This was logged in Phase 6 multi-team review as "Aria + Iris co-review F150 content next sprint." Now done.

---

## Method

Six articles. Two reviewers. Each article gets a Brand pass (voice, banned-language, register, tone) and a UX pass (persona-fit, emotional beat, what does the user actually do with this, would Sofia screenshot it and send to her therapist). Where they disagreed, they wrote it out and reconciled.

Sofia 34 was the primary persona used in the review (late-diagnosed last year, ADHD inattentive, working in tech, in therapy).

---

## Per-article verdict + change applied

### Article 1 — Title change + paired sentence about what just changed in the user's head

**Before**
> Title: *Understanding your diagnosis*
> Body: "You weren't lazy. You weren't broken. The system missed you, and now you have a name for it."

**Iris:** "Title sounds like a textbook chapter. The user just got told they have ADHD; they want company, not a chapter heading."
**Aria:** "Body lands. But it stops at the diagnosis being real — doesn't tell them what changed in their head."

**After**
> Title: *You found a name for it.*
> Body: "You weren't lazy. You weren't broken. The system missed you, and today you have a name for the shape of how your brain works. The first thing this changes: the story you've been telling yourself about yourself was wrong on the most important part."

The title became a sentence. The body stays direct, then adds the "what just shifted" beat — that the self-narrative was incorrect on the most important part. That's the felt sense of the first night after diagnosis, named.

---

### Article 2 — Forward-looking beat added

**Before**
> "Late diagnosis triggers both. The grief is for the years that could have been easier. The relief is everything you no longer have to make up about yourself."

**Iris:** "Solid framing. But 'years that could have been easier' compounds regret without giving an exit. Sofia would close her eyes and just sit with that. Not what we want."
**Aria:** "Add the forward-looking beat. What's the work of the next few months?"

**After**
> "Late diagnosis triggers grief for the years a name would have made easier, and relief for everything you no longer have to make up about yourself. Both are real. Neither cancels the other. The work of the next few months is letting them sit side by side without one demanding to win."

"Demanding to win" — Aria's phrasing. Names what the user is actually doing in their head when grief and relief alternate. Gives a forward action: let them sit side by side.

---

### Article 3 — Three concrete examples replacing one-line abstraction

**Before**
> "The choices that didn't make sense then make sense now. Not as excuses — as data."

**Iris:** "Too short. The user has 30+ years of decisions to re-read. One sentence doesn't carry it. Give me three concrete examples — concrete, not therapy-speak."
**Aria:** "Three good examples. Not 'I struggled to focus' — actual scenes."

**After**
> "The choices that didn't make sense then make sense now. Not as excuses — as data. Examples: the job you couldn't start until the deadline panic kicked in; the friendship that drifted because you didn't return the third text; the room you cleaned for six hours when you had three other things due. Each of those was your nervous system doing its actual job, not you failing at someone else's."

Three scenes any late-diagnosed adult will recognize. The "your nervous system doing its actual job" line is the reframe — closes the article.

---

### Article 4 — Actual script line added

**Before**
> "Some will receive it; some won't. Both responses are about them, not you."

**Iris:** "True. But Sofia came here looking for words to say to her mom. We told her a true thing about reactions; we didn't give her the words. She'll close the article still not knowing what to text."
**Aria:** "Give her a script. Don't pre-defend, don't ask permission, state it."

**After**
> "Some will receive it; some won't. Both responses are about them, not you. A script that travels well: \"I just got an ADHD diagnosis. It explains a lot of things I've struggled with for years. I'm not asking you to do anything with this — I just wanted you to know.\" Don't pre-defend. Don't ask permission. State it. Their first reaction isn't their final one."

The script is verbatim usable. "Their first reaction isn't their final one" is the permission to send the text without needing the perfect response.

---

### Article 5 — Permission line added so the gut-punch doesn't spiral

**Before**
> "You spent decades performing being 'fine.' That cost is real. Recovery starts with letting yourself be inconsistent on purpose."

**Iris:** "This is the most emotionally charged article. 'Decades performing being fine' is a gut-punch — it lands hard. But we send Sofia into a spiral if we don't immediately follow with permission."
**Aria:** "Add: you don't have to fix this in a day. Make the first week small."

**After**
> "You spent decades performing being \"fine.\" That cost is real. The exhaustion you've described as \"just stress\" is often the receipt. Recovery isn't a single decision; it's letting yourself be inconsistent on purpose, in small places, before you try it in the big ones. You don't have to dismantle this in a day. The first week is just noticing where you're still performing."

The "exhaustion is the receipt" connects the abstract cost to a felt experience. "First week is just noticing" makes the next action small enough to actually do.

---

### Article 6 — Three concrete tactics replacing "advocate hard"

**Before**
> "The system assumed boys. It still does. Bring data, advocate hard, find providers who get it."

**Iris:** "'Advocate hard' is filler. Tell Sofia HOW. What does she actually do at the appointment?"
**Aria:** "Three concrete tactics. Specific enough that someone could execute them at their next visit."

**After**
> "The system assumed boys. It still does. Three concrete tactics: (1) Bring your prospective data — DRSP, ASRS-5, ADHD-RS scores from this app are exactly the patient-self-report a thoughtful clinician will respect. (2) Ask specifically: \"Have you treated adult women with ADHD before?\" — this is a yes/no that filters fast. (3) If a provider says \"you're functioning fine, you can't have ADHD,\" they don't understand masking, and you're allowed to find another one. You don't owe anyone the labor of educating them."

Tactic 1 connects back into the app — the user's own data is leverage. Tactic 2 is a conversational filter Sofia can use in five seconds. Tactic 3 names the exact line a wrong provider says, and gives permission to leave. "You don't owe anyone the labor of educating them" closes the article on autonomy.
