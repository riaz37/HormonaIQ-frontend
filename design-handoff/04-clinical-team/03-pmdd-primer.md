## The One-Paragraph Version

PMDD (Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder) is a severe, cyclical mood disorder that affects 3–8% of women who menstruate. For approximately 7–10 days before every period, women with PMDD experience debilitating depression, rage, anxiety, dissociation, and suicidal thoughts — symptoms so severe they cannot work, maintain relationships, or function. Then their period arrives, and within 24–48 hours, the symptoms disappear completely. This happens every single month. For years. For decades. The cause is a documented neurobiological abnormality — not a personality flaw, not bad stress management, not hormonal imbalance. The brain of a person with PMDD responds abnormally to normal progesterone fluctuations. It is a DSM-5 depressive disorder. It is real. It is treatable. And 8–12 years pass on average before a woman gets diagnosed.

---

## Part 1: What Is PMDD?

PMDD stands for Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder. The word "dysphoric" means profoundly unhappy or uneasy — it comes from the Greek for "hard to bear." The American Psychiatric Association added PMDD to the DSM-5 (the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders) in 2013 as a standalone depressive disorder — not as a subtype of PMS, not as a mood disorder NOS, but as a discrete, diagnosable condition with its own criteria.

Before 2013, PMDD appeared in the DSM's appendix as a condition "requiring further research." For the millions of women who had it, this meant: not real enough to treat, not recognized enough to diagnose, not serious enough for insurance to cover. That changed in 2013. The clinical reality did not change — the women had PMDD throughout. The institutional recognition changed.

### What It Is Not

PMDD is not:
- **Severe PMS.** PMS is common (up to 75% of women have some premenstrual symptoms). PMDD affects 3–8% and causes functional impairment severe enough to disrupt daily life. The clinical threshold is the difference.
- **A hormonal imbalance.** Women with PMDD do not have abnormal hormone levels. Their estrogen, progesterone, LH, FSH are all normal. What is abnormal is how their brains respond to normal hormonal fluctuations.
- **A mental health condition that just happens to worsen before periods.** That is Premenstrual Exacerbation (PME) — a different condition where an underlying disorder (depression, bipolar, anxiety) worsens premenstrually. PMDD has no underlying disorder. It exists only in the luteal phase and disappears completely in the follicular phase.
- **Something that can be fixed by yoga, journaling, or evening primrose oil.** These may help some symptoms at the margins. They do not treat the underlying neurobiological cause.

## Part 2: Why Does PMDD Happen? (The Biology)

This is the most important thing to understand, and the thing most women with PMDD have never been told.

### The Hormone Is Normal. The Brain's Response Is Not.

After ovulation, progesterone rises throughout the luteal phase. Progesterone is metabolized by the body into a compound called **allopregnanolone (ALLO)**. Allopregnanolone modulates GABA-A receptors in the brain — the same receptors that benzodiazepines (anti-anxiety drugs like Valium) act on. In most women, rising ALLO during the luteal phase has a calming, anxiolytic effect.

In women with PMDD, the brain's GABA-A receptors respond to allopregnanolone abnormally. Instead of producing calm, the same neurochemical produces anxiety, dysphoria, and emotional dysregulation. The exact mechanism is still being studied, but the leading hypothesis involves abnormal GABA-A receptor subunit composition in women with PMDD — the receptor is physically different, so the same chemical produces the opposite effect.

This is why PMDD feels like a neurological event to the people who have it — because it is. This is also why SSRIs (antidepressants) work for PMDD even when taken only 14 days per month: SSRIs appear to modulate ALLO's effect on GABA-A receptors, not to increase serotonin in the conventional antidepressant sense.

**Key takeaway for the product:** The app must never frame PMDD as something that lifestyle changes will fix. It must frame the luteal phase as a neurobiological event — not a character flaw, not a motivation failure, not something the user can willpower through.

### The Cyclical Pattern Is Diagnostic

PMDD symptoms are strictly time-locked to the luteal phase (approximately the 2 weeks between ovulation and the start of menstruation). They begin within a few days of ovulation, peak in the last 5–7 days before menstruation, and resolve within 24–48 hours of the period starting.

The diagnostic tell is the **follicular-phase symptom-free window** — days 4 through 10 after the period starts. In this window, women with PMDD feel completely well. Normal. Often strikingly positive. This is not mania — it is the absence of the neurobiological dysregulation that defines the luteal phase.

This pattern — sick, then well, then sick again — repeating every cycle — is what distinguishes PMDD from major depression, bipolar disorder, or generalized anxiety disorder, all of which have persistent symptom profiles. The cyclical nature is the proof of diagnosis. And proving it requires tracking.

## Part 3: Who Gets PMDD?

- **Prevalence:** 3–8% of women who menstruate. That is approximately 5.5 million women in the United States alone, 800,000 in Australia, and roughly 150–200 million globally.
- **Age of onset:** Usually begins in the late teens to mid-twenties, though it can start at any point after the first menstrual cycle.
- **Lifetime course:** PMDD typically worsens in severity in the late 20s and 30s, may briefly improve with pregnancy, and often dramatically worsens during perimenopause — the hormonal volatility of the perimenopausal transition intensifies PMDD in most patients. Symptoms resolve at menopause (when cycles stop).
- **Who is at higher risk:**
  - Women with a personal history of depression, anxiety, or trauma (though PMDD can occur without any of these)
  - Women with ADHD (~45% of women with ADHD meet PMDD criteria)
  - Women with a family history of PMDD (genetic component confirmed)
  - Women with history of sexual trauma or PTSD (though trauma does not cause PMDD — it may lower the threshold)
- **Comorbidities:** PCOS (HR 1.54 — PCOS users are 54% more likely to develop PMDD), ADHD, endometriosis, hypothyroidism, migraine with aura

## Part 4: What Are the Symptoms?

The DSM-5 requires at least 5 of the following 11 symptoms, present in the majority of cycles, with at least 1 from the first 4 (core mood symptoms):

### Core Mood Symptoms (at least 1 required)
1. **Marked affective lability** — sudden sadness, tearfulness, extreme sensitivity to perceived rejection
2. **Marked irritability or anger** — persistent rage, disproportionate anger, increased interpersonal conflict
3. **Markedly depressed mood** — hopelessness, self-deprecating thoughts, a sense that life is not worth living
4. **Marked anxiety or tension** — feeling "keyed up," on edge, unable to relax

### Additional Symptoms
5. Decreased interest in usual activities — hobbies, friends, work, sex
6. Difficulty concentrating — brain fog, inability to complete tasks
7. Lethargy and fatigue — bone-deep exhaustion, not tiredness
8. Change in appetite — specific food cravings (usually carbohydrates), overeating
9. Sleep disruption — either too much (hypersomnia) or insomnia
10. Feeling overwhelmed and out of control
11. Physical symptoms — breast tenderness, bloating, joint/muscle pain, headaches, lower back pain

### What This Actually Looks Like

Clinical language does not convey what PMDD feels like from the inside. Here are direct quotes from r/PMDD (3.2 million members):

> "I feel like a completely different person for two weeks every month. During PMDD I hate everything, I hate myself, I want to disappear. Then my period comes and I look back at what I said and thought and I'm horrified. It's not me. But it was me."

> "My PMDD destroyed my marriage. My husband thought I was emotionally abusive. I thought so too. It took 14 years to get a diagnosis."

> "The suicidal thoughts are the scariest part. I know they're coming — I can feel them coming — and I can't stop them. I've called 988 three times. Each time, my period started the next day and I was fine."

> "I've lost three jobs. Not because I'm bad at my job — I'm excellent at my job for three weeks out of four. The fourth week I can't get out of bed."

### Suicidal Ideation — The Statistic Nobody Talks About

**39–82% of women with PMDD experience suicidal ideation during the luteal phase.** In a qualitative study of 32 PMDD patients, 26% had attempted suicide. These are not women with pre-existing depression. These are women whose suicidality is cyclically triggered by a neurobiological event, recedes with menstruation, and returns the next month.

This is the most critical clinical safety consideration in the product. Any app that tracks PMDD without a safety protocol is clinically negligent.

## Part 5: How Is PMDD Diagnosed?

Diagnosis requires **prospective confirmation over two complete menstrual cycles**. This means the patient must track their symptoms daily — every single day, not just the bad days — for two months before a clinician can confirm the diagnosis.

This is not bureaucratic delay. It is clinical necessity. Retrospective recall of symptoms is demonstrably unreliable — studies show that women recalling symptoms from memory overestimate follicular-phase severity and misattribute luteal symptoms to other causes. The only valid diagnostic data is prospective (real-time) tracking.

### The Clinical Instrument: The DRSP

The gold standard instrument for PMDD diagnosis is the **Daily Record of Severity of Problems (DRSP)** — a 24-item validated questionnaire completed daily on a 1–6 scale. Items cover all 11 DSM-5 symptom domains plus three functional impact domains (work, relationships, social activities).

After two cycles of DRSP data, clinicians apply the **C-PASS (Carolina Premenstrual Assessment Scoring System)** to determine whether the pattern meets PMDD criteria.

No existing consumer app implements the DRSP. Paper charts and spreadsheet templates sell for $5–20 on Etsy. Women are managing a medical condition with artisan stationery.

### The PMDD vs. PME Distinction (Critical for Treatment)

Approximately **40% of women who seek PMDD treatment actually have PME** — Premenstrual Exacerbation of another disorder (depression, bipolar II, PTSD, anxiety). The distinction matters enormously for treatment:
- **PMDD:** Responds to luteal-phase intermittent SSRI dosing (14 days on, 14 days off)
- **PME:** Requires continuous SSRI dosing plus treatment of the underlying condition

An app that only tracks luteal-phase symptoms cannot make this distinction. A proper PMDD app must track the follicular phase with equal rigor — the symptom-free follicular window is the diagnostic differentiator.

## Part 6: How Is PMDD Treated?

PMDD is treatable. This is important to state clearly because many women with PMDD spend years believing it is not.

### First-Line Treatments

**1. SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors)**
The most evidence-backed treatment for PMDD. Work differently in PMDD than in depression — the mechanism is primarily GABA-A receptor modulation via ALLO, not serotonin reuptake inhibition. Can be dosed:
- **Continuously** (daily, like traditional antidepressants)
- **Luteal-phase intermittent** (day 14 to day 1 of next period, then stop)
- **Symptom-onset** (started when symptoms begin, stopped at period onset)

Luteal-phase dosing works as well as continuous dosing for most PMDD patients — a significant quality-of-life advantage (no daily medication, fewer cumulative side effects).

**2. Drospirenone-containing OCP (Oral Contraceptive Pills)**
Drospirenone is a progestogen with anti-mineralocorticoid properties that appear to blunt the ALLO dysregulation. Yaz (drospirenone 3mg + ethinyl estradiol 20mcg) is FDA-approved specifically for PMDD. Australian clinicians additionally use Zoely (nomegestrol acetate + 17β-estradiol) as first-line.

Note: Not all OCPs work for PMDD. Some (particularly levonorgestrel-containing pills) can worsen PMDD symptoms. The formulation matters.

### Second-Line Treatments

**3. GnRH Agonists** (e.g., Lupron/leuprorelin) — Temporarily suppress ovarian function, eliminating the hormonal cycle that triggers PMDD. Highly effective but significant side effects (induced menopause, bone density loss). Used for severe, treatment-resistant PMDD.

**4. Transdermal oestradiol patches** (UK RCOG guideline) — Dampens the ovulatory LH surge, reducing the amplitude of hormonal fluctuation. Used with progestogen add-back to protect the endometrium.

### Psychological Treatments

**CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy)** has historically been recommended for coping skills. A 2025 Australian study (Oliveri et al.) published the first DBT-informed treatment model for PMDD, arguing that DBT's acceptance framework is a better fit than CBT's cognitive restructuring — because PMDD symptoms are biologically real, not cognitively distorted.

### Lifestyle Interventions

Limited but real evidence for: aerobic exercise (3×/week), calcium and magnesium supplementation, reduced caffeine and alcohol (especially in luteal phase), vitamin D supplementation in deficient patients. These are adjuncts to treatment, not substitutes.

## Part 7: What Does Living With PMDD Actually Look Like?

Understanding the lived experience is essential for building a product that users actually trust.

### The Cycle of PMDD

**Week 1–2 (Follicular phase, post-period):** User feels well, often remarkably well. Clear-headed, productive, connected. She may doubt she has PMDD at all — "maybe I was just stressed last month." She is the person she recognizes as herself.

**Week 3 (Early luteal, post-ovulation):** Subtle shifts begin. Sleep disrupts. Appetite changes. Edges of irritability appear. For many women this is undetectable; for others it is the early warning window.

**Week 4 (Late luteal, PMDD peak, days 22–28):** The neurobiological dysregulation peaks. Depression, rage, dissociation, exhaustion, suicidal ideation. Relationships fracture. Work performance collapses. The user may say or do things she will deeply regret. She knows on some level this is PMDD — but knowing it is neurobiological does not stop the experience.

**Period onset:** Within 24–48 hours of menstruation beginning, symptoms lift. The clarity of the follicular phase returns. With it comes the "follicular remorse" — the user looks back at the past week with horror and tries to repair what was damaged.

**Then it happens again next month.**

This cycle — sick, well, sick again — is what PMDD patients describe as the most psychologically taxing aspect. Not any single luteal phase, but the knowledge that every month, on a predictable schedule, they will lose two weeks of their life.

## Part 8: The Care Gap — Why This Problem Is Unsolved

### Diagnosis Delay: 8–12 Years in Australia, 10–20 Years Globally

The average woman with PMDD sees **5.1 healthcare providers** before receiving a diagnosis. In Australia the average diagnostic delay is 8–12 years; globally the historical figure is 10–20 years.

Why?
- **Provider education gaps.** PMDD is inadequately covered in most medical curricula.
- **Dismissal language.** Women are told "that's just PMS," "you're too sensitive," "have you tried exercise?"
- **Retroactive recall limitations.** Without a tracking instrument, a woman describing her symptoms from memory presents inconsistently — her luteal-phase distress doesn't match her follicular-phase composure. The cyclical pattern is invisible without data.
- **Mental health stigma compounds hormonal stigma.** Bringing up suicidal ideation that happens monthly requires trust most women don't have in a 10-minute GP appointment.
- **No validated consumer tracking tool exists.** Paper charts and spreadsheets are the state of the art. Women manage a cyclically debilitating medical condition with a printable PDF from a hospital website.

### What Exists Today

| Tool | What It Does | What's Missing |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| Me v PMDD (app) | Basic symptom logging | No DRSP, no C-PASS, no physician report |
| PMDD Tracker (app) | Mood diary with cycle overlay | No clinical validation, no evidence-based content |
| Belle Health | PMDD tracking with ring hardware | No DRSP, no PME distinction, no safety protocol |
| Clue | Cycle tracking with PMDD mode | Not clinically validated, no luteal-specific depth |
| Etsy spreadsheet templates | Manual DRSP grids | No automation, no insights, no safety net |
| Nothing | — | A clinical-grade, safety-aware, DRSP-implementing, C-PASS-scoring PMDD companion |

A 2024 JMIR study (Beddig et al., PMC11687174) involving 18 PMDD patients in co-design workshops found that existing apps: (a) do not implement validated instruments, (b) lack safety features, (c) do not differentiate PMDD from PME, (d) have no understanding of the follicular-phase experience, and (e) are built without clinical input.

## Part 9: What HormonaIQ Tracks — And Why Every Field Exists

This is the product rationale: not what we built, but why we built each piece.

### Why We Log Every Day (Not Just Luteal Days)

PMDD diagnosis requires demonstrating the follicular-phase symptom-free interval. Tracking only luteal days is medically insufficient — it cannot distinguish PMDD from PME, and it cannot generate a valid C-PASS score. Every day of the cycle is a data point.

### Why We Use the DRSP Scale (1–6, Not 1–5)

The DRSP is the only validated instrument for prospective PMDD diagnosis that generates data usable in clinical documentation. The 1–6 scale (not 1–5 or 1–10) is the validated instrument's actual scale. Using a different scale means the data is not compatible with clinical literature benchmarks or C-PASS scoring.

### Why We Track Mood, Physical Symptoms, Cognition, and Function Separately

DRSP Criterion B (functional impairment) is required for PMDD diagnosis. Tracking symptoms alone is insufficient — the physician report must show that symptoms impaired work, relationships, and social functioning. Without the functional impact fields, the physician report cannot support a formal diagnosis.

### Why We Track the Follicular Phase

The follicular symptom-free window is the diagnostic proof of PMDD. Seven days of low-scoring follicular-phase data — days 4 through 10 after period onset — is the clinical evidence that symptoms are luteal-specific, not continuous.

### Why We Have a Safety Module

39–82% of PMDD patients experience suicidal ideation during the luteal peak. An app that tracks PMDD and provides no safety pathway is a liability and a failure of duty of care. The safety module is not a legal add-on — it is a core clinical feature.

### Why We Track Triggers

Caffeine, alcohol, poor sleep, high-stress events, and dietary quality all interact with PMDD severity. Individual-level trigger identification — "your PMDD scores are consistently 2 points higher the day after alcohol" — is the kind of insight that changes behavior because it comes from the user's own data, not generic health advice.

### Why We Have an Anti-Anchoring UX Rule

Clinical research on symptom tracking instruments shows that users anchor to yesterday's score when they can see it during entry. The DRSP must be completed without viewing prior entries. This is not a UX preference — it is a measurement validity requirement. An anchored dataset systematically underestimates PMDD severity and causes false negatives in C-PASS scoring.

### Why We Never Show Mood-Lifting Tips During Luteal Peak

Sending a notification suggesting a walk when a user has logged suicidal ideation scores of 4 is not supportive. It is dismissive. It is the app version of "have you tried yoga?" — the phrase PMDD patients hear most and despise most. During any day when safety scores are elevated, the interface shows only: validation + safety resources + the user's own data.

### Why We Need Two Complete Cycles Before Generating a Report

DSM-5 Criterion D requires prospective confirmation over at least two consecutive symptomatic cycles. Generating a diagnostic-pattern report on one cycle's data is clinically invalid and potentially harmful (false confidence in an incomplete dataset).

If HormonaIQ delivers:
- A woman experiencing PMDD symptoms for the first time can begin tracking and arrive at a clinician appointment with two cycles of DRSP data that instantly contextualizes her presentation
- A woman who has spent 10 years being told "it's just PMS" has clinical documentation proving a cyclical pattern with functional impairment

## Part 13: Features We Will NEVER Build for PMDD Users

These are permanent exclusions. They are not "on the roadmap later" — they are clinical and ethical decisions.

| Feature | Why We Won't Build It |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Calorie counting or macro tracking** | PCOS + PMDD populations have 4× elevated eating disorder rates. Calorie displays are a direct pathway to harm. We track food quality for PMDD users only in terms of trigger identification (caffeine, sugar, alcohol). |
| **Streak counters or gamification** | A user in PMDD crisis who misses 3 days of logging does not need a broken streak counter when she returns. Neurobiological impairment penalized by gamification is a design failure. |
| **Mood-lifting tips during luteal peak** | "Have you tried a walk?" sent when a user has logged suicidal ideation of 4 is not support — it is dismissal. During any elevated safety score, the interface shows validation + safety resources + data. Never tips. |
| **Ovulation prediction framing** | Ovulation is not the goal of PMDD tracking — the luteal phase that follows it is. Framing the fertile window as something to "optimize" is tone-deaf for this population. Ovulation is tracked as a phase anchor, not a celebration. |
| **Retroactive diagnosis from backdated entries** | Retrospective symptom recall is unreliable. If a user backdates two weeks of entries, C-PASS is not run on that data. Backdated entries are clearly marked in the physician report and excluded from diagnostic calculations. |
| **"It's common" normalization without safety context** | "Many women with PMDD experience suicidal ideation" without an immediate safety resource attached is dangerous dismissal. Every prevalence statistic about difficult symptoms is paired with what the user can do. |
| **Cycle phase as spiritual identity** | "Your dark feminine phase" or "inner critic season" aestheticizes a medical condition. Plain language only: "You are in late luteal. This is typically a higher-symptom window based on your history." |
| **Partner dashboard or passive partner access** | Partners do not passively receive cycle data. The user can export and share a summary — but access is always user-initiated, never passive. |
| **Pregnancy tracking** | Out of scope. If a user becomes pregnant, we surface a message and suggest appropriate alternatives. No pregnancy module. |
| **Advertising or data monetization** | Subscription only. No advertising SDKs. No health data to ad networks. The Flo FTC settlement exists for a reason. |
| **BMI calculation or display** | Clinically contested and harmful for this population. Weight is tracked as a metabolic trend signal if the user opts in — never as a BMI input. |
