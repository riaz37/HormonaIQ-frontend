**Based on:** ESHRE 2022 Guidelines, ACOG 2026 CPG, CMAJ 2023, WHO data, NICE NG73, multiple 2024-2025 meta-analyses

## Part 1: What Is Endometriosis

Endometriosis is a chronic, systemic, estrogen-dependent inflammatory disease in which tissue similar to the endometrium (the lining of the uterus) grows outside the uterus. This tissue responds to hormonal changes across the menstrual cycle — thickening, breaking down, and bleeding — but has no route of exit from the body. The result is inflammation, scarring, adhesions, and often severe pain.

**What it is NOT:**
- Not just "bad periods." Dysmenorrhea from endometriosis is qualitatively different from primary dysmenorrhea — it is progressive, often incapacitating, and does not improve with age without treatment.
- Not always visible on standard imaging. Superficial peritoneal endometriosis cannot be seen on ultrasound or MRI. Most clinical guidelines (ESHRE 2022) now allow empirical clinical diagnosis without requiring laparoscopy for uncomplicated presentations.
- Not psychosomatic. Endometriosis is a histologically confirmed disease with measurable inflammatory markers and documented structural changes. Women's pain has been historically dismissed as psychological — this is a care failure, not a clinical finding.
- Not rare. Endometriosis affects approximately **190 million women and girls worldwide** (WHO) — roughly 10% of all women of reproductive age. It is more common than diabetes in women. Yet it receives a fraction of the research funding.

**The diagnostic delay problem:**
The average time from first symptoms to confirmed diagnosis is **7–12 years** across global studies. A 2024 University of York study found the UK average is now 8 years and 10 months — up from 8 years in 2020, meaning the delay is getting worse, not better. 58% of women in the UK visit their GP more than ten times before receiving any investigation. In the US, patients require an average of 7 provider visits. Adolescents wait an average of 23 months just to be referred after first seeking care.

The diagnosis delay is not primarily a patient awareness problem. It is a systemic clinical failure: the normalisation of menstrual pain, inadequate training in primary care (fewer than 2 hours of menopause/endometriosis education in most medical curricula), absence of reliable non-surgical biomarkers, and symptom overlap with IBS and other common conditions.

## Part 2: Types of Endometriosis

Endometriosis is not a single uniform condition. It has four distinct anatomical presentations, each with different symptom profiles, diagnostic requirements, and treatment approaches.

### Type 1: Superficial Peritoneal Endometriosis
- **Description:** Lesions on the surface of the peritoneum (the membrane lining the abdominal and pelvic cavities). Most common type.
- **Appearance:** Variable — red/pink (active), black/brown (old blood — "powder burns"), white/clear (early or healed lesions)
- **Key features:** Cannot be reliably detected by ultrasound or MRI. Laparoscopy required for diagnosis. Not correlated with pain severity — some women with extensive superficial disease have minimal pain; some with one or two lesions have severe incapacitating pain.
- **Relevant staging:** rASRM Stages I–II predominantly

### Type 2: Ovarian Endometriosis (Endometrioma)
- **Description:** Endometriotic cysts within the ovary filled with old blood ("chocolate cysts" — dark brown, thick fluid).
- **Key features:** Detectable by ultrasound with high sensitivity/specificity. Reduces ovarian reserve (AMH). Can rupture (acute pelvic pain). Recurrence common after surgery.
- **Clinical impact:** Associated with infertility. Cystectomy preferred over drainage. Affects 17–44% of women with endometriosis.
- **Relevant staging:** rASRM Stages III–IV (size and bilaterality determine points)

### Type 3: Deep Infiltrating Endometriosis (DIE)
- **Description:** Lesions that penetrate more than 5mm below the peritoneal surface into pelvic organs.
- **Key structures involved:** Uterosacral ligaments (most common), rectovaginal septum, rectosigmoid colon, bladder, ureters, pouch of Douglas.
- **Key features:** Most symptomatic form. Associated with dyschezia, deep dyspareunia, rectal pressure, cyclical rectal bleeding, dysuria, flank pain. Advanced ultrasound and MRI can detect with >90% sensitivity/specificity.
- **Clinical impact:** May require multidisciplinary surgical team (colorectal surgeon, urologist). Ureteral DIE can cause silent hydronephrosis and kidney loss — a medical emergency.
- **Relevant staging:** ENZIAN / #ENZIAN classification preferred over rASRM for this type

### Type 4: Adenomyosis
- **Description:** Endometrial glands and stroma within the myometrium (uterine muscle wall) itself. Classified separately but co-occurs in 15–90% of endometriosis patients (depending on diagnostic method used).
- **Key features:** Diagnosed by MRI or ultrasound (uterine thickening, heterogeneous myometrium, myometrial cysts). Presents with heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB), dysmenorrhea, and uterine tenderness. Can worsen fertility independently. Included in #ENZIAN as the "A" category.
- **Hysterectomy is curative** for adenomyosis but not for endometriosis (which persists on other structures).

### Extrapelvic / Rare Sites
- **Diaphragm / thoracic:** Cyclical shoulder tip pain (radiates to shoulder during menstruation), cyclical chest pain, haemoptysis, catamenial pneumothorax. Rare but serious.
- **Umbilical:** Cyclical umbilical pain and bleeding.
- **Surgical scar:** Post-C-section scar endometriosis. Cyclical pain at scar.
- **Brain, peripheral nerves:** Very rare. Cyclical neurological symptoms.

## Part 3: Who Is Affected and Why

### Prevalence
- ~10% of all women of reproductive age (roughly 190 million worldwide)
- Up to 30–50% of women with infertility
- 45–82% of women with chronic pelvic pain (CPP)
- 40–50% of adolescents and young adults with persistent pelvic pain
- Peaks: can develop from menarche through menopause; most diagnosed in late 20s–early 30s (after years of delay)

### Risk Factors (Established)
- Family history: 3–15× increased risk in first-degree relatives; 50% heritability in twin studies
- Early menarche
- Short menstrual cycles (<27 days)
- Heavy or prolonged menstrual flow
- Low birth weight
- Müllerian anomalies (structural uterine abnormalities)
- Nulliparity
- Low BMI

### Protective Factors (Associated, Not Causal)
- Exercise (moderate — evidence is mixed)
- Omega-3 rich diet
- Full-term pregnancies
- Breastfeeding

### Pathophysiology — Why It Happens
No single cause is established. Multiple mechanisms contribute:
1. **Retrograde menstruation** (Sampson's theory): Most widely accepted. Menstrual blood flows backward through the fallopian tubes into the pelvis. Occurs in ~90% of women; most have immune systems that clear the cells. In women with endometriosis, cells implant and grow.
2. **Coelomic metaplasia**: Peritoneal cells transform into endometrial-like cells (explains extrapelvic disease).
3. **Hematogenous/lymphatic spread**: Explains distant sites (lung, brain).
4. **Stem cell origin**: Bone marrow-derived progenitor cells implanting outside the uterus (emerging theory).

**Contributing mechanisms once established:**
- Local estrogen production by lesions (aromatase overexpression — even without ovarian estrogen, lesions can self-sustain)
- Prostaglandin overproduction → inflammation and pain amplification
- Neurogenesis (new nerve growth into lesions — explains why lesion size does not correlate with pain severity)
- Immune dysregulation (reduced NK cell activity, altered macrophage function)
- Central sensitisation (chronic pain rewires spinal cord and brain pain processing)

## Part 4: The Complete Symptom Map

### The Four Ds — Core Clinical Presentation
These four pain types are the clinical hallmarks. The combination of 2+ of them is highly specific for endometriosis.

| Symptom | Definition | Prevalence | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Dysmenorrhea** | Menstrual cramps, often severe | ~90% of symptomatic patients | Progressive — worsens over time. Mean VAS 5.76. Often the first symptom. "Incapacitating" in a significant proportion. |
| **Dyspareunia** | Pain during or after sex | 40–50% | Two subtypes: deep (uterosacral/rectovaginal lesions) and superficial (pelvic floor hypertonicity). Deep dyspareunia = "hitting pain in the upper vagina." |
| **Dyschezia** | Pain on defecation | Common in bowel/DIE | Often cyclical (worse with period). Rectal pressure. Associated with rectosigmoid involvement. |
| **Dysuria** | Pain on urination | ~1–2% bladder involvement | Often cyclical. Associated with bladder endometriosis. Cyclical hematuria is a red flag. |

### Additional Pain Types
| Symptom | Mechanism | Clinical Note |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| **Chronic pelvic pain (CPP)** | Central sensitisation + ongoing inflammation | Present in >50% of Stage III/IV; the "4 Ds" are intermittent but CPP is constant |
| **Ovulation pain (mittelschmerz)** | Periovulatory inflammation | Worsened by endometriomas + pelvic adhesions |
| **Lower back pain** | Uterosacral ligament involvement | Common; misdiagnosed as musculoskeletal |
| **Leg/sciatic pain** | Pelvic nerve impingement | Especially from uterosacral/parametrial DIE |
| **Shoulder tip pain** | Diaphragmatic lesions | Cyclical = red flag for thoracic endometriosis |
| **Flank pain** | Ureteral involvement | Urgently investigate: risk of silent hydronephrosis |
| **Rectal bleeding (cyclical)** | Bowel endometriosis | Bowel investigation required |
| **Hematuria (cyclical)** | Bladder endometriosis | Urology referral required |

### Gastrointestinal Symptoms
- **Bloating** — very common; often cyclical ("endo belly"); can be severe enough to appear pregnant
- **Constipation and/or diarrhoea** — cyclically worse; often misdiagnosed as IBS
- **Nausea and vomiting** — particularly during menstruation
- **Abdominal cramping** — non-menstrual; linked to bowel adhesions or irritation
- **Rectal pressure** — suggests posterior deep disease
- **Altered bowel habits** — persistent pattern changes warrant bowel endometriosis assessment

**Key IBS differentiator for tracking:** Endometriosis GI symptoms are **cyclically worse with menstruation**. IBS symptoms are triggered by food, stress, and are non-cyclical. Tracking the relationship between GI symptoms and cycle day is the single most important differentiator.

### Urinary Symptoms
- Frequency and urgency (especially cyclical)
- Pain on urination
- Incomplete bladder emptying
- Cyclical haematuria (blood in urine) — red flag, requires bladder endometriosis assessment

### Bleeding
- Heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) — particularly with adenomyosis co-occurrence
- Prolonged periods (>7 days)
- Intermenstrual spotting
- Clots during menstruation
- Cyclical rectal or vaginal bleeding at sites of endometriosis

### Systemic Symptoms — Often Invisible
| Symptom | Prevalence | Evidence |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Fatigue** | Very high | Associated with inflammation, pain, and sleep disruption; severe enough to cause work impairment |
| **Brain fog** | **80%** (FACT-Cog, 2025) | Cognitive impairment: memory, concentration, processing speed, word-finding. Worse than typical cancer patient samples on perceived cognitive functioning. *The Lancet OGWH* published a dedicated review in 2025. |
| **Sleep disruption** | **70.8%** (2024 meta-analysis) | Insomnia, obstructive sleep apnea, restless legs. Pain increases sleep disturbances 6×. CPP worsens sleep quality 3×. |
| **Depression** | OR **3.61×** vs. controls | At least 1-in-3 have clinical depression. Bidirectional — partly genetic (Yale 2024). |
| **Anxiety** | OR **2.61×** vs. controls | Often misdiagnosed as primary anxiety disorder before endo diagnosis. |

### Adolescent Presentation
- Dysmenorrhea + school absenteeism are key clinical flags
- GI and genitourinary symptoms more prominent than in adults
- Acyclical pelvic pain more common than in adults
- Lesions often appear white/red/clear, not the classic brown "powder burns"
- 23-month average delay from symptom onset to referral in adolescents

## Part 5: Diagnosis

### Why Diagnosis Is Hard
1. **No blood test.** CA-125 has only 54% sensitivity (91% specificity) — useless as a screening test. No approved non-invasive biomarker exists clinically yet (though miRNA testing is approaching approval).
2. **Superficial peritoneal disease is invisible on imaging.** Ultrasound and MRI have >90% sensitivity for endometrioma and DIE but zero sensitivity for superficial peritoneal lesions.
3. **Pain is normalised.** "Periods are supposed to hurt" is one of the most damaging phrases in primary care. Severe dysmenorrhea that affects daily function is never normal.
4. **Symptom overlap.** IBS, interstitial cystitis, pelvic floor dysfunction, and fibromyalgia all share symptoms with endometriosis. Most women are misdiagnosed with one or more of these before receiving correct diagnosis.
5. **Knowledge gap.** Most medical schools include fewer than 2 hours of endometriosis education. Many GPs feel undertrained (BMS 2021 survey: 44%).

### ESHRE 2022 Major Change: Laparoscopy Is No Longer Required
The 2022 European Society of Human Reproduction and Embryology (ESHRE) guidelines represent a fundamental shift: **empirical clinical diagnosis is now acceptable** when symptoms + physical examination + imaging are consistent with endometriosis. Laparoscopy is still the definitive diagnostic tool, but it is no longer required before starting treatment. This change is expected to reduce diagnostic delay significantly.

### Diagnostic Pathway (ESHRE 2022 / ACOG 2026)
1. **Clinical history:** The 4 Ds, cycle pattern, pain progression, family history, impact on daily life
2. **Physical examination:** Pelvic exam, rectovaginal exam (for DIE). Note: normal exam does NOT rule out endometriosis (low diagnostic accuracy)
3. **Imaging:**
   - **Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS)** — first-line. Basic TVUS: diagnoses endometrioma with high accuracy. Advanced (sliding sign, compartment-specific assessment) TVUS: >90% sensitivity/specificity for DIE
   - **MRI** — for complex DIE, surgical planning, bowel/bladder involvement. Also excellent for adenomyosis. >90% sensitivity/specificity for DIE
   - **Transabdominal/transrectal ultrasound** — for adolescents and women without previous intercourse
4. **Laparoscopy with biopsy** — definitive. Indicated when: imaging negative + empirical treatment fails, or pre-surgical planning required
5. **Biomarkers** — CA-125 is adjunct only. miRNA testing approaching clinical availability.

### Clinical Red Flags Requiring Urgent Assessment
- Cyclical shoulder tip pain → thoracic/diaphragmatic endometriosis (rare but serious)
- Cyclical hematuria → bladder endometriosis
- Cyclical rectal bleeding → bowel endometriosis
- Flank pain → ureteral involvement, hydronephrosis risk
- Rapidly enlarging adnexal mass → rule out malignancy

### Staging Systems

#### rASRM Staging (Surgical — Laparoscopic Assessment)
Points are assigned based on lesion location, depth, size, and adhesion type:
- **Stage I (Minimal):** 1–5 points
- **Stage II (Mild):** 6–15 points
- **Stage III (Moderate):** 16–40 points
- **Stage IV (Severe):** >40 points

**Critical limitation:** Stage does NOT correlate with pain severity. A woman with Stage I can have severe incapacitating pain; Stage IV can have minimal symptoms. rASRM also inadequately describes DIE — it was designed in 1979 and revised in 1996; the clinical community increasingly considers it outdated.

#### #ENZIAN Classification (2021 — Comprehensive)
Published in *Acta Obstet Gynecol Scandinavica* (Keckstein et al., 2021). Endorsed by ESGE, ESHRE, and World Endometriosis Society. The most anatomically complete staging system. Covers:
- **P** — Peritoneal disease (superficial)
- **O** — Ovarian endometrioma
- **T** — Anatomical distortion (tube/ovary)
- **A** — Adenomyosis (diffuse/focal)
- **B** — Uterosacral ligaments / pelvic wall (L/R)
- **C** — Rectovaginal septum
- **D** — Rectosigmoid colon
- **FA** — Adenomyosis (grade)
- **FB** — Bladder
- **FU** — Ureter
- **FI** — Intestine distal to sigmoid
- **FO** — Other (diaphragm, nerve, scar)

Each compartment graded 1–4 by size/depth of invasion. #ENZIAN is MRI-compatible, making it usable for non-surgical staging.

#### Endometriosis Fertility Index (EFI)
Score 0–10 (higher = better post-surgical fertility prognosis). Combines patient history (age, duration of infertility, prior pregnancies) with surgical findings. Does NOT assess pain.

## Part 6: Clinical Measurement Instruments

### EHP-30 (Endometriosis Health Profile-30)
**Gold standard quality-of-life instrument for endometriosis.** Developed at Oxford (Jones et al., 2001). Validated in 56 language versions. Recommended by ASRM and ESHRE for all clinical trials. FDA-compliant.

**Core instrument — 30 items across 5 subscales:**

| Subscale | Items | Content |
|----------|-------|---------|
| **Pain** | **11** | Functional limitations from pain: unable to go to social events, do housework, go to work, stand, sit, walk, etc. |
| **Control and powerlessness** | **6** | Feeling of control over life with symptoms; symptoms "ruling your life" |
| **Emotional well-being** | **6** | Mood, psychological state, mood swings |
| **Social support** | **4** | Relationships, feeling understood, social connections |
| **Self-image** | **3** | Body perception, confidence, self-worth |

**Response scale:** Never (0) / Rarely (1) / Sometimes (2) / Often (3) / Always (4)
**Scoring:** Each scale 0–100 (lower = better QoL). Scale score = (raw score sum ÷ max possible raw score) × 100
**Completion time:** Under 15 minutes

**6 optional supplementary modules (23 additional items):**
1. Work (5 items)
2. Relationship with children (2 items)
3. Sexual relationship / functioning (5 items)
4. Feelings about medical profession (4 items)
5. Feelings about treatment (3 items)
6. Feelings about infertility (4 items)

**Psychometric properties:** Cronbach's alpha >0.83 across all subscales. ICC for test-retest: 0.88–0.98. Highly responsive to treatment change (most responsive domain: "Control and Powerlessness").

### EHP-5 (Short Form)
5 core items (one per subscale) + 6 modular items (one per optional module). Same response scale and 0–100 scoring. Completion time: ~3 minutes. Preferred for time-constrained clinical settings and HormonaIQ daily/weekly use.

### Biberoglu and Behrman (B&B) Scale
The most widely used clinical trial instrument before EHP-30. 3 symptom items + 2 physical sign items, all scored 0–3.

**Symptom items (patient-reported, 4-week recall):**

| Item | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|------|---|---|---|---|
| Dysmenorrhea | None | Mild | Moderate | Severe (incapacitating) |
| Dyspareunia | None | Mild | Moderate | Severe (prevents intercourse) |
| Chronic pelvic pain | None | Mild | Moderate | Severe (incapacitating) |

**Symptom summary:** 0 = None / 1–3 = Mild / 4–6 = Moderate / 7–9 = Severe

**Physical sign items (clinician-assessed):**
- Pelvic tenderness: 0–3
- Induration: 0–3

**Limitations:** Not formally validated for reproducibility. ASRM recommends as secondary endpoint only. Inadequate when patient is amenorrhoeic or not sexually active.

### NRS (Numerical Rating Scale) — ASRM Primary Outcome
**The ASRM-recommended primary endpoint for clinical trials.** Score 0–10 (0 = no pain, 10 = worst imaginable).

**Separate NRS assessments are required for each pain type:**
- NRS Dysmenorrhea
- NRS Non-menstrual / Chronic pelvic pain
- NRS Dyspareunia
- NRS Dyschezia
- NRS Dysuria

**Severity classification:**
- 0: None
- 1–3: Mild
- 4–7: Moderate
- 8–10: Severe

**MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference) on VAS:** 10mm. Clinical trials use ≥30% reduction from baseline as responder criterion.

### ESD (Endometriosis Symptom Diary)
Daily diary (24-hour recall period). Originally developed by AbbVie for elagolix trials. FDA-compliant.
- Daily NRS pain ratings for each type
- Body diagram for pain location
- Daily bleeding assessment (none/spotting/light/normal/heavy)
- Medication tracking (scheduled and breakthrough)
- Bloating rating

### EIS (Endometriosis Impact Scale)
Weekly impact tool (7-day recall). 32 items. 5-point verbal scale: Not at all / Slightly / Moderately / A lot / Extremely (+ Does not apply).
Domains: Physical activities, emotional well-being, sexual activities
Sub-domains: Work/study, social/leisure, household tasks, sleep, concentration

## Part 7: Treatment

### First-Line Medical Treatment

**NSAIDs (First Step)**
- Ibuprofen, naproxen, mefenamic acid, diclofenac
- Mechanism: reduce prostaglandin synthesis → reduce inflammation and cramping
- Evidence: effective for dysmenorrhea; no evidence for non-menstrual pain
- Best used: start 1–2 days before expected period onset and continue through bleeding
- Limitation: long-term NSAID use carries GI and cardiovascular risks

**Combined Hormonal Contraceptives (CHC)**
- Pill (monophasic), vaginal ring, transdermal patch
- Mechanism: suppress ovulation, reduce retrograde menstruation, thin endometrium
- Key: use **continuously** (skip placebo pills) — significantly more effective than cyclic use
- Evidence: 5 RCTs; reduce dysmenorrhea, non-cyclic pelvic pain, dyspareunia; improve QoL
- Adverse effects: nausea, spotting, headache, mood changes, breast discomfort

**Progestins (Preferred First-Line by Many Guidelines)**

| Medication | Route | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Dienogest 2mg** | Oral daily | 4th-generation; approved for endometriosis in EU, Asia, Australia; best evidence base; superior long-term efficacy |
| Norethindrone acetate 5mg | Oral | 0.5–3 tablets daily; widely used |
| Norethindrone 0.35mg | Oral (mini-pill) | 1–3 tablets daily |
| Desogestrel 75μg | Oral | Often reliably suppresses ovulation; good for dysmenorrhea |
| Depot MPA (DMPA) | IM injection | 150mg every 6-8 weeks; effective but affects bone density with long-term use |
| LNG-IUS (Mirena 52mg) | Intrauterine | Local effect; reduces dysmenorrhea and HMB; ESHRE-recommended |
| Etonogestrel implant | Subdermal | Some evidence for pain reduction |

### Second-Line Medical Treatment

**GnRH Agonists (Medical Menopause)**
- Leuprolide acetate (3.75mg monthly or 11.25mg 3-monthly IM)
- Goserelin acetate (3.6mg monthly SC)
- Nafarelin (nasal spray, 200μg twice daily)
- Mechanism: pituitary desensitisation → complete estrogen suppression
- Highly effective but require **add-back hormone therapy** to prevent bone loss and manage menopausal symptoms
- **Initial flare effect:** pain worsens for 2–4 weeks before improving — warn patients
- Cannot be used >6 months without add-back

**GnRH Antagonists (Newer Oral Class — No Flare)**
- Mechanism: direct pituitary blockade — immediate, dose-dependent estrogen suppression without initial flare
- **Elagolix (Orilissa):** 150mg once daily (partial suppression) or 200mg twice daily (near-complete). FDA-approved. Significantly reduces dysmenorrhea, CPP, dyspareunia.
- **Relugolix (Myfembree):** 40mg/day; combination with estradiol 1mg + norethindrone 0.5mg allows 24-week use with maintained efficacy and better tolerability
- **Linzagolix:** 75mg/day alone (moderate pain) or 200mg/day with add-back (severe pain). Late-stage trials.
- 2025 meta-analysis: oral GnRH antagonists effective for endometriosis pain; dose-dependent efficacy and safety

**Add-Back Therapy (For Both GnRH Agonists and High-Dose Antagonists)**
- Purpose: prevent bone density loss, manage vasomotor symptoms, extend treatment duration
- Standard: low-dose estrogen (estradiol 0.5–1mg) + progestogen
- Does not reduce efficacy of treatment for endometriosis

**Aromatase Inhibitors (Third-Line)**
- Letrozole 2.5mg/day or anastrozole 1mg/day
- For refractory pain not responding to standard treatments
- Must be combined with hormonal suppression
- Evidence: observational studies + small RCTs only

### Surgical Treatment

**Laparoscopic Excision (Standard of Care)**
- Complete removal of lesion including the root (margins of healthy tissue)
- Recurrence at 1 year: **5–17%** (vs. 37% for ablation — Cochrane 2024)
- Long-term recurrence: 22% at 2 years, 40–50% at 5 years (all surgeries without hormonal suppression)
- Evidence: consistently superior to ablation for pain recurrence and fertility

**Laparoscopic Ablation (Burning / Vaporisation)**
- Destroys surface only — roots remain
- 40–60% recurrence within 1–2 years
- Appropriate only for superficial lesions when excision is not feasible
- Patients should be informed excision is preferred

**Ovarian Endometrioma (Cystectomy)**
- Cyst wall excision preferred over drainage and coagulation
- Reduces recurrence; carries risk to ovarian reserve
- Counsel on AMH impact before surgery

**Post-Surgical Hormonal Suppression**
- **Reduces recurrence by 59%** (RR 0.41)
- GnRH agonist 6 months post-surgery: 16.4% recurrence vs. 28.9% without treatment
- ESHRE: strongly recommended for patients not immediately seeking pregnancy
- HormonaIQ should prompt post-surgical treatment tracking and response monitoring

**Hysterectomy**
- Option for select patients (completed childbearing + severe symptoms)
- **Does not cure endometriosis** (unlike adenomyosis). Lesions outside the uterus persist.
- With bilateral oophorectomy: marginally better pain outcomes but triggers surgical menopause

### Non-Pharmacological Treatment

**Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy (PFPT)**
- RCT evidence (2024): significantly improves urinary, bowel, and sexual function in DIE patients
- Addresses hypertonic pelvic floor (almost universal in chronic endo pain)
- Techniques: manual therapy, myofascial release, biofeedback, TENS, stretching, breathing
- ESHRE 2022: included in recommended treatment algorithm

**Dietary Interventions**
- **Low-FODMAP diet:** RCT evidence (2024) — 60% response rate vs. 26% for control diet; significantly reduces GI symptoms, bloating, abdominal pain in endo+GI symptom patients
- **Anti-inflammatory diet (Mediterranean-style):** reduces pain and QoL scores after 6 months; women with pro-inflammatory diet are 4× more likely to have endometriosis
- **Omega-3 fatty acids:** protective effect in two prospective studies
- **Reduce:** red/processed meat, trans fats, excessive alcohol, excessive caffeine, refined sugar
- **Fruit fibre:** associated with reduced endometriosis risk in multiple studies

**Mind-Body Therapies**
- CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy): moderate evidence for pain management and QoL
- ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy): emerging evidence
- Mindfulness-based therapy: growing evidence base
- Yoga, TENS, heat therapy: adjunct symptom management

## Part 8: Comorbidities and Co-Occurring Conditions

Endometriosis is increasingly understood as a systemic, whole-body condition. The comorbidity burden is significant and under-recognised.

### Mental Health
| Condition | Risk vs. Controls | Evidence |
|-----------|------------------|---------|
| **Depression** | **OR 3.61** (95% CI 3.32–3.92) | 2024 large epidemiological study, n=8,000+ |
| **Anxiety** | **OR 2.61** (95% CI 2.30–2.97) | Same study |
| **Eating disorders** | **OR 2.94** (95% CI 1.96–4.41) | Genetic predisposition confirmed (Yale 2024) |

Key point: the depression and anxiety are **not purely a consequence of chronic pain**. Yale 2024 genetic study confirmed shared genetic predisposition — the association is bidirectional and partly independent of pain severity.

### IBS and Gastrointestinal
- **3.5× more likely** to have IBS history
- Meta-analysis: >2× risk vs. controls
- Shared mechanism: visceral hypersensitivity, central sensitisation, gut microbiome dysbiosis
- Clinical implication: rule out endometriosis in all women diagnosed with IBS

### Sleep
- **70.8% prevalence** of sleep disturbance (2024 meta-analysis, n=7 studies)
- Pain increases sleep disturbances ~6×; CPP worsens sleep quality >3×
- Types: insomnia (most common), OSA, restless legs syndrome
- Tracking tool: Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI)

### Cognitive Function
- **80%** of endometriosis patients show cognitive impairment on FACT-Cog (2025)
- Worse than typical cancer patient scores on perceived cognitive functioning
- *The Lancet OGWH* 2025: dedicated paper on brain fog as "the overlooked symptom"
- Mechanisms: neuroinflammation, disrupted brain connectivity, pain-attention interference

### Chronic Fatigue / ME/CFS
- **2.79× higher odds** of developing ME/CFS (2025 meta-analysis)
- Prevalence of ME/CFS in endo patients: 4–74% across studies
- Shared mechanisms: immune dysregulation, chronic inflammation

### Autoimmune Conditions
- 2× overall risk of autoimmune diagnosis vs. matched controls
- **Hypothyroidism: 7× more common** (9.6% vs. 1.5% general population)
- Elevated risk: SLE, Sjögren's syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis, IBD (Crohn's + UC)
- Oxford 2025: confirmed shared genetic link between endometriosis and immune conditions

### Fibromyalgia
- **2× more likely** than general female population
- Shared central sensitisation mechanism
- May co-occur as a distinct condition or represent overlapping pathology

### Pelvic Floor Dysfunction
- Hypertonic pelvic floor in the vast majority of patients with chronic endo pain
- Co-occurs with vulvodynia, pudendal neuralgia, interstitial cystitis
- Both deep and superficial dyspareunia components often present

### Adenomyosis
- Co-occurs in **15–90%** of endometriosis patients
- Compounds HMB, dysmenorrhea, and infertility
- Requires separate tracking and treatment consideration

### Infertility
- 30–50% of infertile women have endometriosis
- 30–50% of endo patients experience infertility
- Endometrioma specifically reduces ovarian reserve (AMH)
- IVF success rates: positive pregnancy rate ~80%, live birth rate ~61% in optimised protocols

## Part 9: The Care Gap — Why This Problem Is Unsolved

### The Diagnosis Delay
Global average 7–12 years from symptom onset to confirmed diagnosis. Leading causes:

**1. "Periods are supposed to hurt":** The normalisation of menstrual pain in clinical and social contexts is the primary driver of delay. Severe, progressive dysmenorrhea affecting daily function is never normal and should always prompt investigation.

**2. No reliable non-invasive test:** There is no blood test that confirms endometriosis. CA-125 is inadequate as a screening tool. miRNA testing is approaching approval but not yet available clinically. Ultrasound and MRI cannot detect superficial peritoneal disease.

**3. The surgical requirement (historical):** Until ESHRE 2022, laparoscopy was considered the diagnostic gold standard, meaning diagnosis required surgery. Many clinicians and institutions still operate under this model, unnecessarily delaying treatment initiation.

**4. GP knowledge gap:** BMS 2021 survey: 44% of GPs felt undertrained in menopause management. Most medical school curricula include fewer than 2 hours on menstrual disorders. Endometriosis specialists are rare and geographically concentrated.

**5. Symptom overlap with other conditions:** 58% of endo patients are previously diagnosed with IBS, depression, anxiety, or pelvic floor dysfunction before correct diagnosis. These diagnoses are not wrong — they often co-exist — but they delay the primary diagnosis.

**6. Adolescent dismissal:** 23-month average delay from first symptoms to referral in teenagers. School absenteeism dismissed as avoidance; severe cramps dismissed as "normal teenager pain."

**7. Race and ethnicity:** Black women are significantly less likely to be referred to gynaecology, less likely to be offered medical management, and more likely to be diagnosed with psychiatric conditions rather than gynaecological ones. Documented and persistent health inequity.

**8. Economic burden invisibility:** Global annual cost of endometriosis: **$119 billion**. Yet it receives a fraction of research funding compared to conditions with smaller population impact. The economic argument for early diagnosis and treatment is overwhelming and largely ignored.

### The Case for Tracking

Phendo (15,000+ users) demonstrated that patient-generated tracking data can produce research-grade datasets that bridge the gap between patient experience and clinical characterisation. The same principle applies in reverse: if a user has 6 months of NRS-scored dysmenorrhea, dyschezia, and dyspareunia data — with cycle-correlated bowel symptoms and PSQI-tracked sleep — she walks into her GP appointment with clinical-grade evidence that is significantly harder to dismiss than a verbal report of "bad periods."

## Part 10: What Living with Endometriosis Actually Looks Like

### At Diagnosis (Often Years Too Late)

*"I'm 28. I've had bad periods since I was 14. Three GPs told me it was normal. One told me to try the pill. I was diagnosed with IBS at 21. A different doctor told me I had anxiety. Last year, a new gynaecologist did an ultrasound and found a 6cm endometrioma on my left ovary. She said I'd probably had endometriosis for at least a decade. I spent 10 years being told my pain was in my head."*

The majority of endometriosis patients have this story in some form. The diagnosis is not a beginning — it is a belated confirmation of what the patient already knew. The emotional response is often simultaneously relief (finally, an explanation) and grief (for all the years lost, the relationships strained, the careers limited).

### With Deep Infiltrating Endometriosis

*"My period pain starts 5 days before my period and doesn't end until 4 days after. During my period I can't defecate without screaming. Sex has been off the table for two years. My husband tries to understand. Last month I spent 4 days unable to work. My boss thinks I'm exaggerating."*

DIE symptoms are often the most severe and the most misunderstood. The combination of dyschezia, deep dyspareunia, and rectal pressure is so distinctive it should raise immediate clinical suspicion — but often doesn't because clinicians aren't expecting it.

### As an Adolescent

*"I'm 16. I missed 23 days of school this year because of my period. My mum has taken me to the GP four times. The first time they gave me ibuprofen. The second time they said it was growing pains. The third time they referred me to a psychiatrist. I'm not depressed. I'm in pain."*

The adolescent experience is distinct: symptoms often present as severe dysmenorrhea + school absence + GI symptoms, against a backdrop of adults who assume all teenage girls have painful periods.

### With the Invisible Symptoms

*"I don't have a period right now because I'm on the injection. My endometriosis is 'managed.' But I can't remember the last time I felt rested. I can't concentrate at work. I cancelled three dinners last month because I was too tired. My friends think I'm antisocial. My GP thinks I'm depressed. I think my brain is on fire."*

Fatigue, brain fog, and cognitive impairment are the symptoms least likely to be connected to endometriosis in clinical settings and the most likely to be misattributed to psychiatric conditions.

## Part 12: What Changes If This Works

The average endometriosis patient accumulates 7–12 years of inadequate care, multiple misdiagnoses, significant mental health burden, and (for a third to half) infertility. The economic cost is $119 billion per year globally. The suffering is not quantifiable.

A clinically-grounded tracking tool does not cure endometriosis. But it changes three things:

**1. The evidence available at each clinical encounter.** Instead of describing 10 years of pain from memory, a woman walks in with 6 months of NRS-scored daily pain data, EHP-30 quality-of-life scores, cyclical symptom patterns, and treatment response tracking. The clinical conversation changes from "when did the pain start?" to "here is the data — what do we do next?"

**2. The diagnostic delay.** Early, structured symptom capture can bring the average 7–12 year delay forward. The ESHRE 2022 shift toward empirical diagnosis means a structured symptom record is now clinically sufficient for diagnosis in many presentations. A woman who has been tracking for 3 months has a clinical record; a woman relying on memory has anecdotes.

**3. The comorbidity landscape.** Depression, sleep disruption, cognitive impairment, IBS, and thyroid disease all go undetected longer than they should in endometriosis patients because clinicians and patients focus exclusively on the pain. HormonaIQ monitors all of these as first-class tracked outcomes — giving women a complete picture of their health and enabling targeted treatment across all dimensions of the disease.
