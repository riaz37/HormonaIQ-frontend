## Part 1: What Is ADHD

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder is a neurodevelopmental condition characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interfere significantly with daily functioning across multiple settings. It is not a deficit of attention per se — people with ADHD can sustain intense attention on tasks they find interesting or urgent — but rather a deficit in **self-regulation of attention**: the ability to direct, sustain, and redirect attention according to intention rather than impulse or environmental salience.

### The Neuroscience: What Is Actually Happening

ADHD is driven by dysregulated neurotransmission in dopamine and norepinephrine systems. The core brain circuits involved include:

**The mesoaccumbens pathway** (ventral tegmental area → nucleus accumbens): governs reward processing, motivation, and the anticipation of future rewards. In ADHD, this pathway shows:
- **Lower dopamine D2/D3 receptor density** in the accumbens and midbrain
- **Reduced dopamine transporter concentrations** in the same regions
- A fundamental shift in reward sensitivity that makes future rewards feel distant and abstract, and immediate rewards feel urgent

**The mesocortical and nigrostriatal pathways** (prefrontal cortex, basal ganglia, caudate): govern executive function, working memory, impulse control, and action monitoring. These systems are underactivated in ADHD, producing deficits in planning, task initiation, sequencing, and self-monitoring.

**Important clarification:** ADHD is NOT a simple dopamine deficiency. It is a dopamine *dysregulation* — the system functions differently, not merely at lower levels. This distinction matters for treatment: stimulant medications increase dopamine availability in these circuits, but the response is calibrated, not simply "more dopamine = better."

### The Interest-Based Nervous System

Russell Barkley's framework articulates this as an **interest-based nervous system**: the ADHD brain requires interest, challenge, novelty, or urgency to generate the dopamine signal needed for task activation. This produces the paradox familiar to every person with ADHD — they can spend 6 hours in hyperfocus on a video game while unable to spend 20 minutes on a necessary task. The difference is not willpower. It is neurobiological task activation circuitry.

### What ADHD Is Not

- Not a character flaw, laziness, or low intelligence
- Not caused by poor parenting, screen time, or diet (though diet may modulate severity in some cases)
- Not something people "grow out of" — approximately 65% of children diagnosed with ADHD continue to meet criteria as adults; 90% show continued functional impairment even when full DSM criteria are no longer met
- Not rare: global adult prevalence is **3.1% (95% CI 2.6-3.6%)**; US adult prevalence is **~6.0% (approximately 15.5 million adults)**

## Part 2: ADHD in Women — The Diagnostic Equity Problem

### The Numbers

| Metric | Boys/Men | Girls/Women |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Childhood diagnosis rate | 13% | 6% |
| Adolescent medication rate | 74.8% | 25.2% |
| Mean age at diagnosis | 11.2–22.7 years | 16.3–28.6 years |
| Diagnostic delay vs. male peers | — | ~4 years longer |
| Comorbid anxiety prevalence | 32% | 53% |
| Comorbid depression prevalence | 14% | 29% |
| PMDD comorbidity | — | ~46% |

*Sources: CHADD, Monash University 2026, multiple US health databases*

The male-to-female diagnostic ratio is **2:1 in childhood** — but it narrows to **1.6:1 in adulthood**. This narrowing does not represent women developing ADHD in adulthood. It represents the accumulated recognition of cases that should have been caught earlier. A 2026 Monash University study concluded that **sex-based disparities in ADHD prevalence rates may reflect misdiagnosis and underdiagnosis of females rather than true male biological disposition to ADHD.**

### Why Women Are Missed

**1. Symptom presentation differences**  
Girls and women with ADHD typically present with **inattentive symptoms** (forgetfulness, disorganization, difficulty sustaining mental effort, internal restlessness) rather than **externalized hyperactivity** (inability to sit still, disrupting class, blurting out). Hyperactive behaviors trigger referrals. Inattentive girls sit quietly in class, appear compliant, and struggle invisibly.

**2. Diagnostic criteria bias**  
Only **21% of participants in DSM-5 field trials were girls**. Diagnostic criteria were developed predominantly from studies of hyperactive boys. The criterion "often leaves seat in situations when remaining seated is expected" describes a boy in 1970s classroom research. It does not describe a woman in her thirties losing her train of thought mid-sentence, missing three appointments in a week, and crying at 2am because she cannot make herself do the thing she needs to do.

**3. Masking and camouflaging**  
Women with ADHD develop highly sophisticated masking strategies — appearing organized, keeping copious lists, over-preparing for meetings, arriving early to compensate for time blindness, developing external systems that simulate executive function. This masking conceals the disorder from clinicians, teachers, employers, and partners — but not from the woman experiencing it. The internal chaos is constant. The effort to hide it is exhausting. Masking is associated with:
- Higher depression and anxiety rates (independent of ADHD severity)
- Burnout and exhaustion
- Identity confusion
- Lower life satisfaction and quality of life

**4. Comorbidity confounding**  
The higher rates of anxiety and depression in ADHD women create a clinical trap: providers diagnose the comorbidity (anxiety, depression, borderline personality features, eating disorder) and miss the underlying ADHD. The woman is treated for anxiety for a decade while the neurodevelopmental root cause goes unaddressed.

**5. Clinician bias**  
Multiple studies document that clinicians are less likely to consider ADHD when evaluating women showing equivalent symptom burden to men. Gender stereotypes ("ADHD is a boy thing") shape clinical pattern recognition in ways that disadvantage female patients.

### The Late Diagnosis Experience

Women diagnosed with ADHD in their 30s, 40s, and 50s consistently describe a bifurcated reaction:

*Immediate relief* — "My entire life finally makes sense. I'm not lazy. I'm not broken. There is a neurological explanation for everything."

*Subsequent grief* — "I spent 25 years thinking I was fundamentally defective. I ruined relationships, failed courses, lost jobs. All of it was untreated ADHD."

This grief-relief duality is clinically significant. Late diagnosis is not just a delayed positive outcome — it represents decades of compounded harm: accumulated self-blame, missed educational and career opportunities, damaged relationships, substance misuse as self-medication, and mental health burden that might have been prevented. The diagnostic delay is not a benign delay. It is a public health failure.

## Part 3: DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

### Three Presentations

**ADHD — Predominantly Inattentive Presentation (ADHD-I)**  
≥5 symptoms of inattention for ≥6 months; <5 symptoms of hyperactivity-impulsivity

**ADHD — Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive Presentation (ADHD-HI)**  
≥5 symptoms of hyperactivity-impulsivity for ≥6 months; <5 symptoms of inattention

**ADHD — Combined Presentation (ADHD-C)**  
≥5 symptoms of BOTH inattention AND hyperactivity-impulsivity for ≥6 months

*Note: In adults, the threshold is ≥5 symptoms (reduced from the 6 required for children/adolescents)*

### Inattention Criteria (9 items — ≥5 required in adults)

1. Often fails to give close attention to details or makes careless mistakes in schoolwork, work, or other activities  
2. Often has difficulty sustaining attention in tasks or play activities  
3. Often does not seem to listen when spoken to directly  
4. Often does not follow through on instructions and fails to finish schoolwork, chores, or duties (not from defiant behavior or failure to understand)  
5. Often has difficulty organizing tasks and activities  
6. Often avoids, dislikes, or is reluctant to engage in tasks that require sustained mental effort  
7. Often loses things necessary for tasks or activities  
8. Is often easily distracted by extraneous stimuli  
9. Is often forgetful in daily activities  

### Hyperactivity-Impulsivity Criteria (9 items — ≥5 required in adults)

1. Often fidgets with or taps hands or feet, or squirms in seat  
2. Often leaves seat in situations when remaining seated is expected  
3. Often runs about or climbs in situations where it is inappropriate (adults: may be limited to feeling restless)  
4. Often unable to play or engage in leisure activities quietly  
5. Is often "on the go," acting as if "driven by a motor"  
6. Often talks excessively  
7. Often blurts out an answer before a question has been completed  
8. Often has difficulty waiting their turn  
9. Often interrupts or intrudes on others  

### Universal Requirements

All three presentations require:
- Symptoms present in **≥2 settings** (home, school, work, social, other activities)
- Symptoms cause **clinically significant impairment** in social, academic, or occupational functioning
- Symptoms are **not better explained** by another mental disorder
- **Onset of symptoms before age 12** (some symptoms must have been present before 12)
- Symptoms must have persisted for ≥6 months

### Adult vs. Childhood Criteria — Critical Differences

The DSM-5 adult threshold (≥5 vs. ≥6 for children) reflects that ADHD presentations change with age: pure hyperactivity often shifts to internalized restlessness; impulsivity may become more verbal and emotional than behavioral. Adult ADHD frequently manifests as:
- **Time blindness** (inability to perceive time passing accurately)
- **Executive dysfunction** (difficulty starting tasks, transitioning between tasks, completing sequences)
- **Emotional dysregulation** (intense emotional responses, especially to frustration and rejection)
- **Internal hyperactivity** (racing thoughts, inability to "turn off" the brain, restlessness experienced internally)
- **Inconsistency** (performing well one day, completely unable to function the next)

## Part 4: Complete Symptom Map

### 4.1 Core Attention and Executive Function Symptoms

| Symptom Domain | What It Actually Looks Like in Daily Life |
|----------------|-------------------------------------------|
| Inattention | Starting 7 tasks and finishing 0; losing 30 minutes to a phone check; reading the same paragraph 5 times without retention; "mind going blank" mid-sentence |
| Working memory deficit | Forgetting what was said 30 seconds ago; losing the train of thought in your own sentence; needing to re-read instructions multiple times; forgetting to eat |
| Time blindness | Being late despite trying hard to be on time; hours disappearing unnoticed; unable to feel how much time has passed; future deadlines feel unreal until they're today |
| Task initiation paralysis | Knowing exactly what needs to be done and being completely unable to start it; freezing in front of a blank page for hours; the thing not getting started until 11pm the night before |
| Organization | Horizontal surfaces covered in piles that have meaning; losing things that were just in hand; inability to file things systematically; cluttered digital and physical environment |
| Sustained attention | Attention wandering despite trying to focus; needing external pressure (deadline, another person present) to sustain engagement; reading long documents while mentally elsewhere |
| Prioritization | Treating all tasks as equally urgent or unurgent; unable to identify what matters most; doing the easiest tasks while avoiding the important ones |
| Task completion | Dozens of partially-completed tasks across multiple domains; strong starts that stall after the novelty fades |

### 4.2 Hyperactivity and Impulsivity Symptoms (Women-Specific Presentation)

| Symptom | Typical Male Presentation | Typical Female/Adult Presentation |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Hyperactivity | Physically unable to sit still; running, climbing, disrupting class | Internal racing thoughts; leg jiggling; talking rapidly; difficulty sitting in long meetings; channel-surfing through tasks mentally |
| Impulsivity | Blurting out in class; physical aggression; impulsive actions | Impulsive spending; saying things before thinking; interrupting conversations; sending regretted messages; making impulsive decisions in relationships |
| Restlessness | Visible physical agitation | Constant internal buzzing; inability to relax; inability to do "nothing"; feeling lazy when inactive but unable to start |
| Talking | Talking excessively in structured settings | Monologuing when interested in a topic; difficulty moderating conversational volume/pace; thinking out loud |

### 4.3 Emotional Dysregulation

Emotional dysregulation is not listed in the DSM-5 ADHD criteria but is present in **>50% of adults with ADHD** and is often more impairing than the core attention symptoms. It includes:

- **Rapid onset of intense emotional states** — emotions arrive fast and peak hard; there is no gradual build
- **Difficulty modulating the intensity** of emotional responses once triggered
- **Emotional lability** — mood shifts within minutes or hours in response to perceived events
- **Low frustration tolerance** — disproportionate reactions to minor obstacles; giving up when things are hard
- **Irritability** — baseline irritability that is worsened by task demands, transitions, interruptions
- **Shame storms** — cascading negative self-evaluation triggered by mistakes or perceived failures

### 4.4 Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)

**Definition:** Extreme emotional sensitivity and pain — perceived as physical in many cases — triggered by the perception of rejection, criticism, teasing, or falling short of expectations (one's own or others'). RSD is estimated to affect **up to 99% of adults with ADHD**, making it near-universal in the population.

**Key characteristics:**
- Triggered by *perceived* rejection — the rejection does not need to be real or intended
- Emotional pain described as "excruciating," "unbearable," "like a physical punch"
- Onset is instantaneous — there is no warning ramp-up
- Can redirect an entire day's mood and behavior from a single incident
- Peak and then subside — but this can take hours; during which functioning is severely impaired
- Often leads to avoidance of situations where rejection is possible (social withdrawal, not applying for jobs, not trying new things)

**Lived-experience descriptions from research:**
- "I sent one email about a work thing and then couldn't function for the rest of the day waiting for the response"
- "My manager said my presentation was good but suggested one change and I cried in the bathroom for 20 minutes"
- "I replay the conversation for hours afterwards looking for the moment I said the wrong thing"

**RSD is frequently misdiagnosed as:**
- Borderline personality disorder (rejection sensitivity is a feature of both, but RSD is episodic, not pervasive; ego-dystonic in ADHD, ego-syntonic in BPD)
- Bipolar disorder (rapid mood cycling looks like BD)
- Major depressive disorder (low self-worth, rumination, withdrawal)
- Social anxiety disorder (avoidance of social situations)

### 4.5 Hyperfocus and the Burnout Cycle

Hyperfocus is the capacity of the ADHD brain to lock onto a high-interest activity with extraordinary, almost surgical concentration — often for hours at a time with no awareness of time passing. It appears to be the flip side of the attention dysregulation: the same circuit that fails to sustain attention on demand will lock onto interest without voluntary control.

**What makes hyperfocus dangerous:**
- It is involuntary — users cannot reliably enter or exit it on demand
- It leads to neglect of basic needs (eating, drinking, bathroom, sleep, other obligations)
- The post-hyperfocus crash is predictable and severe: sudden exhaustion, inability to do anything, cognitive shutdown
- Repeated hyperfocus-crash cycles progressively deplete reserves
- Burnout from sustained masking + hyperfocus cycles is a documented clinical phenomenon in women with ADHD

**The burnout pattern:**
1. Sustained performance through masking + hyperfocus episodes
2. Gradual accumulation of depleted executive function reserves
3. Trigger event (illness, life stressor, a demand too many)
4. Crash: inability to perform basic tasks, extreme fatigue, inability to mask anymore, emotional breakdown
5. Slow recovery period (weeks to months)
6. Return to masking + performance, and cycle repeats

### 4.6 Time Blindness and Future Blindness

**Time blindness (Russell Barkley, 1990s–present):**  
The inability to perceive time passing accurately in the present moment. People with ADHD have measurably different temporal information processing — the internal clock is unreliable. Consequences:
- Chronic lateness despite genuine effort to be on time
- Hours "disappearing" — "I was just going to check Instagram for a second"
- Inability to pace activity across a day
- Losing track of how long a task has taken or will take

**Future blindness (temporal myopia):**  
The inability to feel the reality of future events, deadlines, and consequences. A 3-week deadline does not feel urgent to the ADHD brain until it is 3 hours away. This is neurological, not character — the future is not emotionally real until it is present. Consequences:
- Chronic deadline crises
- Accumulation of unpaid bills, missed appointments, unfiled paperwork
- Inability to plan, prepare, or save (money, energy, time) for the future
- Described by Barkley as the most impairing aspect of ADHD for adult functioning

### 4.7 Body-Focused Repetitive Behaviors (BFRBs)

**20–38% of individuals with BFRBs** (skin picking/dermatillomania, hair pulling/trichotillomania, nail biting, lip biting, cheek chewing) have comorbid ADHD. The mechanism is dopamine-seeking: when the ADHD brain is understimulated, it finds sensory stimulation through the body. BFRBs also serve as a grounding mechanism during emotional overwhelm.

### 4.8 Sleep

Up to **80% of adults with ADHD** have insomnia or sleep disturbances. The core mechanism is **delayed circadian rhythm**: DLMO (dim-light melatonin onset) is delayed by approximately **90 minutes in adults with ADHD** (45 minutes in children). This means people with ADHD are chronobiologically night owls forced to function in a morning world.

Consequences:
- Difficulty falling asleep before midnight or 1am regardless of desire to sleep earlier
- Extreme difficulty waking in the morning ("sleep inertia" that feels like being under anesthetic)
- "Revenge bedtime procrastination" — staying up late as the only time that feels self-directed
- Daytime fatigue that worsens cognitive function further
- Increased ADHD symptom severity with any sleep disruption

## Part 5: ADHD and Hormones — The Cycle Connection

### The Estrogen-Dopamine Link

Estrogen and dopamine systems are deeply intertwined:
- Estrogen **increases dopamine and serotonin synthesis**
- Estrogen **inhibits monoamine oxidase (MAO)**, reducing dopamine degradation
- Estrogen **enhances prefrontal cortex function** — the brain region most impaired in ADHD
- Estrogen **regulates basal ganglia dopamine neurons** that express estrogen receptors

For women with ADHD — who already have dysregulated dopamine systems — this means that **the menstrual cycle is a cycle of varying symptom severity**. When estrogen is high (follicular phase), ADHD symptoms are relatively better managed. When estrogen falls (luteal phase, pre-menstrually, post-partum, perimenopause), ADHD symptoms worsen.

### Progesterone: The Compounding Factor

Progesterone **reduces dopamine receptor sensitivity**. During the luteal phase (days 15-28), both rising then falling progesterone and falling estrogen create a compounded dopaminergic depression. This is the neurochemical basis for the premenstrual worsening that women with ADHD describe consistently:

- Inattention consistently elevated in mid-luteal and pre-menstrual phases
- Impulsivity increases significantly
- Emotional dysregulation intensifies
- RSD episodes more frequent and more severe
- Medication less effective at the same dose

### Menstrual Cycle Phase Effects — Clinical Research

A 2024 PMC paper (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder and the Menstrual Cycle: Theory and Evidence) documented:
- Estrogen declines predicted **clinically significant 2-fold increases in ADHD inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity symptoms**
- Effect observed in community-recruited women ages 18-25 (non-clinical sample)
- Effect was cyclical and phase-specific, not random

A 2024 review of female-specific pharmacotherapy found:
- **Women respond less strongly to psychostimulant drugs in the luteal phase** despite constant dosing
- **Adjusting stimulant dosages pre-menstrually** improves symptom control
- Some clinicians now recommend **variable dosing strategies** accounting for menstrual cycle phase

### Hormonal Contraception

The interaction between hormonal contraception and ADHD is clinically significant and poorly studied:
- **Oral hormonal birth control** (combined or progestin-only pills) is associated with **increased depression risk** in women with ADHD
- **Non-oral methods** (hormonal IUDs, implants) show better psychiatric outcomes — possibly because they avoid first-pass hepatic metabolism affecting neurotransmitter processing
- **Discontinuing hormonal contraception** can trigger ADHD symptom worsening if estrogen was previously supporting dopamine function
- Clinical recommendation: If a woman with ADHD on hormonal contraception reports mood worsening, evaluate whether the contraceptive formulation is contributing

### Pregnancy and Postpartum

- Women have a **24% higher chance of receiving an ADHD diagnosis** post-pregnancy
- Peak diagnoses occur **2-5 years postpartum**
- Mechanism: Pre-pregnancy, high estrogen supported dopamine; post-partum estrogen drop is dramatic and sudden; ADHD symptoms emerge or worsen visibly
- Postpartum depression and ADHD share symptoms and interact; screening for both is clinically indicated

### Perimenopause and Menopause

This is the most clinically significant hormonal intersection for women with ADHD:

- In a large survey of **2,600+ women aged ≥46 with ADHD, 61% reported ADHD had its greatest impact on daily life between ages 40-59**
- **97% reported symptom exacerbation during menopause**
- Women with ADHD experience **more intense perimenopause symptoms** than non-ADHD women
- **Perimenopause may begin earlier** in women with ADHD (possibly due to cumulative stress and HPA axis dysregulation)

**The perimenopause "double hit":**  
As estrogen declines through perimenopause, the same estrogen-dopamine support system that partially offset ADHD symptoms through the reproductive years withdraws. Women who had "managed" their ADHD through coping strategies and estrogen support now find their compensation mechanisms fail. For many women, perimenopause is when ADHD is finally recognized — the mask slips, and the undiagnosed condition becomes visible.

**Medication response during perimenopause:**  
Many women report that their usual ADHD medication dose becomes less effective during perimenopause. Clinical responses include:
- Higher stimulant doses
- Split dosing (morning + afternoon)
- Addition of HRT (hormone replacement therapy), which, by restoring estrogen, also restores dopamine support
- Combined stimulant + HRT approach often yields superior outcomes to medication adjustment alone

## Part 6: Clinical Assessment Instruments

### 6.1 ASRS v1.1 — Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale

**Developer:** Kessler et al., 2005. Developed for WHO.  
**Format:** 18-item self-report questionnaire  
**Two parts:**
- **Part A (6 items):** Most predictive of ADHD diagnosis. Serves as quick screener. 4+ items endorsed indicates possible ADHD.
- **Part B (12 items):** Additional DSM-based items; supplementary information

**Psychometrics:**
- Internal consistency: Cronbach's α = 0.88
- Sensitivity: 90% | Specificity: 88% (Part A at cutoff 14+)
- AUC: 0.904 (validated in 646 ADHD cases vs 908 controls)
- Recall period: Past 6 months

**Updated version:** ASRS-5 — a 6-item DSM-5 screener with equivalent psychometric properties

**App application:** Monthly screener for symptom monitoring; trend tracking over time; medication response assessment

---

### 6.2 CAARS — Conners' Adult ADHD Rating Scales

**Developer:** Conners, Erhardt & Sparrow, 1999. MHS Assessments.  
**Format:** Available in 3 versions:
- Screening version: 30 items
- Short form: 26 items  
- **Long form: 66 items across 9 subscales**

**Long form subscales:**
1. Inattention/Memory Problems
2. Hyperactivity/Restlessness
3. Impulsivity/Emotional Lability
4. Problems with Self-Concept
5. DSM-IV Inattentive Symptoms
6. DSM-IV Hyperactive-Impulsive Symptoms
7. DSM-IV Total ADHD Symptoms
8. ADHD Index (identifies those who need detailed assessment)
9. Inconsistency Index (response validity)

**Scoring:** T-scores (mean = 50, SD = 10)
- T < 60: Not elevated
- T 60-64: Borderline
- T 65-69: Clinically significant (Elevated)
- T ≥ 70: Very elevated

**Available in:** Self-report and observer/partner versions with separate gender and age-group norms

**App application:** Monthly CAARS-S:S (self-report short form) for symptom monitoring; subscale tracking for targeted treatment response assessment; emotional lability subscale particularly relevant for women's ADHD

---

### 6.3 DIVA-5 — Diagnostic Interview for ADHD in Adults

**Developer:** Kooij & Francken, DIVA Foundation (Netherlands). Successor to DIVA 2.0.  
**Format:** Structured clinician-administered diagnostic interview based on DSM-5

**Structure:**
- Evaluates all 18 DSM-5 ADHD symptom criteria in two domains
- Uses concrete, real-life examples to contextualize each symptom
- Assesses across two time periods:
  - **Current symptoms** (last 6+ months)
  - **Childhood symptoms** (ages 5-12 — required for diagnostic confirmation)
- Impairment assessment in ≥2 life domains required for diagnosis

**Diagnostic algorithm:**
- ≥5 inattention symptoms OR ≥5 hyperactivity-impulsivity symptoms
- Evidence of childhood onset
- Clinically significant impairment in ≥2 domains: work, education, relationships, social, leisure, self-image

**App application:** Not an in-app instrument (requires clinician administration) but serves as reference for the onboarding symptom history questionnaire design

---

### 6.4 ADHD-RS — ADHD Rating Scale

**Developer:** DuPaul et al., 1998. Updated version: ADHD-RS-5.  
**Format:** 18-item scale (9 inattention + 9 hyperactivity-impulsivity)  
**Score range:** 0-54 total (0-27 per subscale)

**Scoring (4-point Likert):**
- 0 = Never or rarely
- 1 = Sometimes
- 2 = Often
- 3 = Very often
**Recall period:** Past 1 month

**Clinical utility:**
- Sensitive to medication effects
- Both clinician-rated and self-report versions
- Standard in medication trials (was the primary endpoint in Vyvanse efficacy studies)

**App application:** Monthly symptom severity tracking; medication response monitoring; comparison against published treatment benchmarks (Vyvanse trials: 19-point ADHD-RS reduction from baseline)

---

### 6.5 WFIRS-S — Weiss Functional Impairment Rating Scale (Self-Report)

**Developer:** Weiss et al., CADDRA.  
**Format:** 50-item questionnaire  

**Six functional domains:**
1. **Family** (relationships with family members, parenting if applicable)
2. **Work/School** (academic/occupational performance, organization, deadlines)
3. **Life Skills** (hygiene, self-care, managing finances, basic responsibility)
4. **Self-Concept** (self-esteem, emotional regulation, confidence)
5. **Social Activities** (peer relationships, maintaining friendships)
6. **Risky Activities** (safety awareness, impulsive consequence)

**Scoring:** 4-point Likert; any item rating 2-3 indicates clinical impairment  
**Optimal cutoff:** WFIRS-S score ≥0.65 classified as ADHD (83% sensitivity, 85% specificity)

**Clinical importance:** Captures functional impairment beyond symptom presence — critical because symptoms and functional impairment are not perfectly correlated. The WFIRS answers "how is ADHD affecting your actual life?" not just "do you have ADHD symptoms?"

**App application:** Monthly functional impact assessment; domain-specific trend tracking; accommodations documentation; physician report functional impairment section

---

### 6.6 Brown EF/A Scales (formerly BADDS)

**Developer:** Dr. Thomas Brown  
**Format:** Self-report scale with T-scores  

**Five subscales mapping to Brown's executive function clusters:**
1. **Activation** — organizing tasks, time estimation, prioritization, getting started
2. **Focus** — sustained attention, filtering distractions, task engagement
3. **Effort** — sustaining motivation, managing fatigue, persisting despite challenges
4. **Emotion** — managing frustration, regulating emotional intensity
5. **Memory** — working memory management, holding and using information

**Clinical value:** Captures the executive function dimensions of ADHD that lie outside the DSM-5 symptom list but are the primary drivers of functional impairment in adults

**App application:** Monthly executive function profile; identifies which EF cluster is most impairing at any given time; informs targeted skill-building recommendations

---

### 6.7 BRIEF-2 — Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function

**Developer:** Gioia, Isquith, Guy & Kenworthy. PAR.  
**Format:** 10-minute behavior rating inventory  

**Three main index scores:**
1. **Behavior Regulation Index** — Inhibit + Self-Monitor subscales
2. **Emotion Regulation Index** — Shift + Emotional Control subscales
3. **Cognitive Regulation Index** — Working Memory + Plan/Organize subscales

**Total: 10 clinical scales** on parent/teacher forms; 9 on self-report

**Scoring:** T-scores with established age norms  
**App application:** Baseline executive function profile; treatment response monitoring; ADHD-perimenopause intersection (emotional regulation subscale particularly relevant during hormonal transitions)

---

### 6.8 BDEFS — Barkley Deficits in Executive Functioning Scale

**Developer:** Russell Barkley  
**Format:** 89 items assessing five executive function problem domains

**Five domains:**
1. **Self-Management to Time** — time blindness, deadline management, time estimation
2. **Self-Organization/Problem Solving** — planning, sequencing, organizing
3. **Self-Restraint** — inhibitory control, impulsive action suppression
4. **Self-Motivation** — sustaining effort, self-motivation for non-preferred tasks
5. **Self-Regulation of Emotion** — emotional self-control, frustration tolerance

**App application:** Self-Management to Time subscale is particularly valuable for HormonaIQ's time blindness tracking feature

## Part 7: Treatment

### 7.1 Stimulant Medications (First-Line)

Stimulants are the most evidence-based ADHD treatment. Meta-analyses show **amphetamines demonstrate superior efficacy compared to methylphenidate** across both adults and children. Response rates: **~70% of adults, ~80% of children**.

**Methylphenidate-class:**
- Medications: Ritalin, Concerta, Biphentin, Medikinet, Metadate
- Mechanism: blocks dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake
- Formulations: immediate-release (4-6h), extended-release (8-12h)
- Titration: start low (typically 5-10mg BID), titrate to effect

**Amphetamine-class:**
- Medications: Adderall, Vyvanse (lisdexamfetamine), Dexedrine
- Mechanism: dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake blockade + release
- **Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) evidence:**
  - RCT n=420 adults: ADHD-RS-IV score reduction of 19 points on average
  - 76.5% subjects improved vs. 23.1% placebo (simulated workplace study n=142)
  - Executive function effect size: 0.74 (p<0.0001)
  - Duration: up to 14 hours
  - Common adverse effects: appetite suppression, insomnia, headache

**Women-specific considerations:**
- Stimulant efficacy varies across the menstrual cycle (lower efficacy in luteal phase)
- Consider variable dosing strategies for luteal phase symptom worsening
- During perimenopause: dose adjustments (higher or split dosing) may be required as estrogen support reduces
- Stimulants + HRT combination often outperforms stimulant dose increase alone

**Required monitoring:**
- Blood pressure and pulse: baseline, 1-3 months after initiation, then every 6-12 months
- Weight/BMI: every 6 months
- Cardiovascular effects at therapeutic doses: +5 bpm heart rate, +2-5 mmHg blood pressure
- ECG: not routine unless personal/family history of arrhythmia or sudden unexplained death

---

### 7.2 Non-Stimulant Medications

**Atomoxetine (Strattera)**
- Class: Selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (NRI)
- FDA-approved; first non-stimulant approved for ADHD
- Dosing: 0.5 mg/kg/day initial (<70 kg); maximum 100 mg/day for adults
- Onset: initial effects 1-4 weeks; full effect 5-8 weeks
- Advantage: no misuse potential; no withdrawal on discontinuation
- Adverse effects: decreased appetite, nausea, abdominal pain, fatigue, dizziness
- Black box warning: suicidal ideation (pediatric)
- Cost: ~$32/month (generic)

**Viloxazine ER (Qelbree)**
- Class: Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI)
- Mechanism: norepinephrine reuptake inhibition + serotonin modulation
- FDA-approved for adults 2023
- Phase 3: symptom reduction 3.7-6.9 points on 54-point scale
- Adverse effects: insomnia, headache
- No significant cardiovascular or hepatic effects
- Cost: ~$330/month (significantly more expensive; relevant for cost tracking)

**Guanfacine ER (Intuniv, Tenex)**
- Class: α2A-adrenergic receptor agonist
- Mechanism: acts on prefrontal cortex post-synaptic receptors to enhance network connectivity
- Dosing: 2 mg/day starting; 4-6 mg/day maintenance
- Phase 3 extension study: significant decrease in all CAARS subscale scores at week 50
- Role: typically reserved for stimulant-intolerant patients; sedating (relevant for sleep)
- Women-specific note: alpha agonists can reduce hot flash frequency — relevant for peri-ADHD overlap

**Bupropion (Wellbutrin) — off-label**
- Class: NDRI (norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor)
- 52% vs 11% placebo response in one RCT; reviews suggest inferior to stimulants
- Clinical niche: comorbid depression + ADHD; patients who cannot tolerate stimulants
- Advantage: may reduce nicotine craving (relevant given 45.9% ADHD-nicotine comorbidity)

---

### 7.3 Non-Pharmacological Treatments

**Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) — Safren Protocol**
The Safren 2010 JAMA trial established CBT efficacy for adults with ADHD with persistent symptoms despite medication:
- Core modules: organization and planning; coping with distractibility; cognitive restructuring
- Optional modules: procrastination management; anger management; communication skills
- Outcomes: significantly lower scores on CGI and ADHD-RS vs. relaxation + education control
- Maintenance: improvements held at 6- and 12-month follow-up with blinded assessors

**Body Doubling**  
Another person's presence (in person or virtual) activates dopamine reward circuitry, dramatically lowering task initiation barriers:
- 2024 study (n=220 neurodivergent participants): 85% reported significant improvement in task completion
- FLOWN study (n=117 suspected/diagnosed ADHD): 2x increase in sustained focus; 30% reduction in anxiety; +2.5 life satisfaction points
- Focusmate: live 1:1 video co-working sessions (25-75 min blocks); 3 free/month
- Mechanism: externalizing motivation (Ramsay, University of Pennsylvania) — longstanding evidence-based strategy

**Exercise**
Aerobic exercise acutely increases dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin; has evidence-based effects on ADHD symptoms comparable to low-dose stimulants in some studies. Recommended: 30-45 minutes aerobic exercise ≥3x/week.

**Sleep Intervention**
- **Melatonin (0.5-3 mg)** timed to DLMO (typically 8-9pm for adults with delayed DLMO)
- **Fixed wake times** regardless of sleep onset time (anchors circadian rhythm)
- **Morning bright light** (2,500-10,000 lux; 30 minutes upon waking)
- **Evening light restriction** — blue light reduction after 8pm

**Nutritional Supplements**
- **Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA + DHA):** 750-2,000 mg/day; evidence for efficacy only at ≥4 months duration; modest effect; EPA dose-dependent correlation
- **Zinc:** Reduces severity in zinc-deficient individuals; may enhance methylphenidate effectiveness (regulates dopamine response); 2004 RCT evidence
- **Magnesium:** Supports neurotransmitter function; effective in deficient individuals; 4-month supplementation showed reduced inattention in one study
- **Iron:** Iron deficiency present in ~84% of ADHD children (vs 18% non-ADHD); supplementation improves symptoms in deficient individuals
- **Vitamin D:** Low levels common in ADHD; supplementation evidence modest

**Pomodoro and Time-Blocking**
- 2021 Journal of Clinical Psychology: time-blocking improved task completion 27% in ADHD adults vs. unstructured work
- Pomodoro adaptation for ADHD: intervals typically extended beyond 25 minutes (ADHD needs longer ramp-up time); highly personalized

## Part 8: Comorbidities

### 8.1 Overall Burden

**~75% of adults with ADHD** have at least one coexisting condition. The clinical pattern in women is particularly complex because comorbidities:
1. Are more prevalent than in male ADHD populations
2. Tend to present first (anxiety, depression) while underlying ADHD is missed
3. Interact with hormonal fluctuations
4. Create multiplicative impairment

### 8.2 Mental Health Comorbidities

| Condition | OR / Prevalence in ADHD | Notes |
|-----------|------------------------|-------|
| Any anxiety disorder | 53% of girls with ADHD (vs 32% boys) | Often presents before ADHD diagnosis; shares neural circuitry |
| Depression | 24.4% females with ADHD vs 13.1% males | Nearly 2x higher in women; often co-occurs with RSD |
| PMDD | ~46% of women with ADHD | Disproportionate impact; shared dopamine dysregulation mechanism |
| Borderline personality features | Elevated in females | RSD + emotional dysregulation pattern; requires differential diagnosis |
| Bipolar disorder | ~20% | Rapid cycling may be RSD; differential diagnosis is critical |
| OCD | Elevated | Shared dopamine pathway; treatment can be complicated |
| PTSD | Elevated | ADHD increases risk exposure; trauma complicates ADHD treatment |

### 8.3 PMDD — The Critical Overlap

Approximately **46% of women with ADHD experience PMDD**. Both conditions involve:
- Dopamine dysregulation (PMDD is serotonin and dopamine dependent)
- Cyclical symptom worsening
- Emotional dysregulation as a core feature
- Response to hormonal manipulation

The clinical consequence: during the luteal phase, women with ADHD + PMDD experience compounding from both conditions simultaneously. ADHD worsens (estrogen falls, dopamine support reduces) and PMDD activates (premenstrual serotonin-dopamine disruption). This creates peak symptom burden in the week before menstruation that is often mistaken for a mood disorder episode.

### 8.4 Autism Spectrum Disorder

- **~41% of children with ASD** have comorbid ADHD
- ADHD is among the most common psychiatric comorbidities in adults with autism
- Shared genetics and neurobiology
- For women: autism-ADHD overlap makes diagnosis of either condition more difficult; both are underdiagnosed in women

### 8.5 Substance Use Disorder

| Substance | Prevalence in Adults with ADHD |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Alcohol | 17-45% (abuse/dependence) |
| Any SUD | 21% of patients with SUD have ADHD |
| Nicotine dependence | 45.9% (vs 27% general population) |
| Cocaine SUD population | 19% ADHD prevalence |

**Self-medication pattern:** 36% of adults with ADHD and alcohol use disorder explicitly identify self-medication as motivation. Caffeine is the most common self-medication (near-universal use for dopamine stimulation).

### 8.6 Sleep Disorders

- **80% of adults with ADHD** have insomnia or sleep disturbances
- **Delayed Sleep Phase Disorder (DSPD):** defined by ≥2-hour delay in sleep-wake cycle; genetically linked to ADHD (shared polymorphisms); **78% of ADHD adults** show delayed sleep-wake timing
- DLMO delayed **~90 minutes** in adults with ADHD
- Sleep disruption worsens ADHD symptom severity the following day — a vicious cycle
- New research framework (Frontiers, 2025): ADHD as a circadian rhythm disorder

### 8.7 Eating Disorders

- **Higher rates in females with ADHD** than males
- **ADHD symptomatology predicts binge eating severity** and bulimic symptoms (controlling for anxiety and depression)
- Impulse control deficits underlie both conditions
- Combined and hyperactive presentations carry highest risk
- Clinical implication: eating disorder screening is important in female ADHD patients

### 8.8 Fibromyalgia and Chronic Pain

- ADHD prevalence in fibromyalgia patients: **24.5-44.7%** across multiple international studies (vs 4-6% general population)
- Shared neurobiological mechanisms: dopamine dysregulation, norepinephrine dysfunction, central sensitization
- "Fibro fog" (cognitive symptoms of fibromyalgia) and ADHD cognitive symptoms overlap significantly
- Women with fibromyalgia + ADHD score higher on functional impairment scales

### 8.9 Metabolic Health

- **~40% higher obesity prevalence** in untreated ADHD students vs. non-ADHD peers
- ADHD is a risk factor for metabolic syndrome components
- Shared mechanism: dopaminergic dysregulation in hypothalamic reward circuitry affects appetite regulation, eating behavior, and metabolic homeostasis
- Insulin resistance: brain insulin resistance disrupts dopamine metabolism; intrauterine growth failure linked to both ADHD and obesity risk
- 2019 meta-analysis: individuals with ADHD designated "high-risk group for cardiometabolic complications"

## Part 9: Daily Life Impact

### 9.1 Relationships

- **>50% of adults with ADHD** struggle to regulate emotions — primary driver of relationship difficulty
- Couples where one partner has ADHD report **2x the dissatisfaction** of neurotypical couples
- Time blindness creates perceived neglect ("You forgot our anniversary again")
- RSD creates reactivity that partners experience as disproportionate or unpredictable
- Hyperfocus-crash cycles can make the ADHD partner seem inconsistent or unreliable
- **~65% of adults with ADHD** report difficulty managing finances; impulsive spending creates relationship conflict

### 9.2 Work and Career

- Workplace accommodations for ADHD are protected under ADA (US), Equality Act (UK), and equivalent legislation in most countries
- Documentation requirements: employer needs confirmation of diagnosis and accommodation needs; not entitled to full medical records
- Common accommodations: flexible scheduling, deadline extension, task breakdown assistance, reduced sensory stimulation
- **Women with ADHD report higher rates of workplace conflict**, burnout, and job loss than males with ADHD

### 9.3 Financial Dysregulation

- **65% of adults with ADHD** report difficulty managing finances
- Lower dopamine drives immediate reward-seeking; time blindness impairs future planning
- Impulsive spending creates debt accumulation and shame cycles that worsen functioning
- ADHD-specific financial support: external accountability systems, automatic payment setup, spending impulse tracking

## Part 11: Lived Experience

### Persona 1 — Sofia, 34. Late-diagnosed, 2 years ago.

"I was a people pleaser at work because I knew if I lost the job I wouldn't get another one. I worked until 11pm every night to cover for the fact that I spent half the workday unable to concentrate. My to-do lists were so detailed they became documents. Everyone told me I was so organized. I was so organized because if I wasn't I would drown.

I got diagnosed when my therapist finally said 'have you ever been assessed for ADHD?' I'd been seeing her for 4 years. 4 years of anxiety treatment that worked slightly but never quite got at the thing underneath.

The thing I want from an app is not another productivity system. I have so many productivity systems. I want something that tracks my brain. I want to see whether I'm worse in the week before my period. I want to show my psychiatrist: this is my CAARS score, this is my medication, this is what happens when estrogen drops. I want to stop explaining myself from scratch every appointment."

---

### Persona 2 — Kezia, 28. Undiagnosed. Suspected.

"I've been told my whole life that I'm smart but I don't apply myself. I finished a science degree by pulling so many all-nighters that I genuinely don't know how I didn't fail. I have 14 browser tabs open right now and I haven't read any of them. I lose my keys daily. I've missed 4 doctors appointments this year.

My gynecologist mentioned I might want to look into ADHD after I described my luteal phase. She said the way I described the week before my period — the complete mental unraveling, the inability to hold anything together — was consistent with what she'd heard from women with undiagnosed ADHD.

I don't have a diagnosis. But I need to track what's happening. I need to be able to walk into an assessment and say: here is what my inattention looks like, here is when it's worst, here is how it affects my work and my relationships. I need evidence. I can't afford to go to an appointment and have someone say 'you don't seem like you have ADHD' because I was so anxious in the appointment that I performed normally."

---

### Persona 3 — Miranda, 46. Diagnosed at 23. Now perimenopausal.

"I've had my ADHD treatment figured out for 20 years. Concerta 36mg, therapy, routines. I was fine. And then I turned 43 and it stopped working. My dose was the same but it was like the medication was getting diluted.

My psychiatrist had never seen this before. Actually, she referred me to an endocrinologist who had. It's the estrogen. She put me on HRT and within a month my medication started working again. It was like coming back to myself.

What I wish existed: an app that would have seen this coming. If I'd been tracking my ADHD symptoms longitudinally and also tracking my cycle changes, the pattern would have been visible months before the crisis. I lost 18 months of functional capacity before someone figured out it was hormonal."
